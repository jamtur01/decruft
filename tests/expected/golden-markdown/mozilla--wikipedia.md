# Mozilla

**Mozilla** is a [free-software](/wiki/Free_software "Free software") community, created in 1998 by members of [Netscape](/wiki/Netscape "Netscape"). The Mozilla community uses, develops, spreads and supports Mozilla products, thereby promoting exclusively free software and open standards, with only minor exceptions.[^1] The community is supported institutionally by the [Mozilla Foundation](/wiki/Mozilla_Foundation "Mozilla Foundation") and its tax-paying subsidiary, the [Mozilla Corporation](/wiki/Mozilla_Corporation "Mozilla Corporation").[^2]

[Mozilla produces many products](/wiki/List_of_Mozilla_products "List of Mozilla products") such as the [Firefox](/wiki/Firefox "Firefox") web browser, [Thunderbird](/wiki/Mozilla_Thunderbird "Mozilla Thunderbird") e-mail client, [Firefox Mobile](/wiki/Firefox_Mobile "Firefox Mobile") web browser, [Firefox OS](/wiki/Firefox_OS "Firefox OS") mobile operating system, [Bugzilla](/wiki/Bugzilla "Bugzilla") bug tracking system and other projects.

## History

On January 23, 1998, Netscape made two announcements: first, that [Netscape Communicator](/wiki/Netscape_Communicator "Netscape Communicator") will be free; second, that the source code will also be free.[^3] One day later, [Jamie Zawinski](/wiki/Jamie_Zawinski "Jamie Zawinski") from Netscape registered mozilla.org.[^4] The project was named Mozilla after the original code name of the [Netscape Navigator](/wiki/Netscape_Navigator "Netscape Navigator") browser which is a blending of "[Mosaic](/wiki/Mosaic_\(web_browser\) "Mosaic (web browser)") and [Godzilla](/wiki/Godzilla "Godzilla")"[^5] and used to co-ordinate the development of the [Mozilla Application Suite](/wiki/Mozilla_Application_Suite "Mozilla Application Suite"), the [open source](/wiki/Open_source "Open source") version of Netscape's internet software, [Netscape Communicator](/wiki/Netscape_Communicator "Netscape Communicator").[^6][^7] Jamie Zawinski says he came up with the name "Mozilla" at a Netscape staff meeting.[^8][^9] A small group of Netscape employees were tasked with coordination of the new community.

Originally, Mozilla aimed to be a technology provider for companies, such as Netscape, who would commercialize their open source code.[^10] When [AOL](/wiki/AOL "AOL") (Netscape's parent company) greatly reduced its involvement with Mozilla in July 2003, the [Mozilla Foundation](/wiki/Mozilla_Foundation "Mozilla Foundation") was designated the legal steward of the project.[^11] Soon after, Mozilla deprecated the Mozilla Suite in favor of creating independent applications for each function, primarily the [Firefox](/wiki/Firefox "Firefox") web browser and the [Thunderbird](/wiki/Mozilla_Thunderbird "Mozilla Thunderbird") email client, and moved to supply them directly to the public.[^12]

Recently, Mozilla's activities have expanded to include Firefox on mobile platforms (primarily [Android](/wiki/Android_\(operating_system\) "Android (operating system)")),[^13] a mobile OS called [Firefox OS](/wiki/Firefox_OS "Firefox OS"),[^14] a web-based identity system called [Mozilla Persona](/wiki/Mozilla_Persona "Mozilla Persona") and a marketplace for HTML5 applications.[^15]

In a report released in November 2012, Mozilla reported that their total revenue for 2011 was $163 million, which was up 33% from $123 million in 2010. Mozilla noted that roughly 85% of their revenue comes from their contract with Google.[^16]

At the end of 2013, Mozilla announced a deal with [Cisco Systems](/wiki/Cisco_Systems "Cisco Systems") whereby Firefox would download and use a Cisco-provided binary build of an open source[^17] [codec](/wiki/Codec "Codec") to play the [proprietary](/wiki/Proprietary_format "Proprietary format") [H.264](/wiki/H.264 "H.264") video format.[^18][^19] As part of the deal, Cisco would pay any patent licensing fees associated with the binaries that it distributes. Mozilla's CTO, [Brendan Eich](/wiki/Brendan_Eich "Brendan Eich"), acknowledged that this is "not a complete solution" and isn't "perfect".[^20] An employee in Mozilla's video formats team, writing in an unofficial capacity, justified[^21] it by the need to maintain their large user base, which would be necessary in future battles for truly free video formats.

In December 2013, Mozilla announced funding for the development of non-[free](/wiki/Free_software "Free software") games[^22] through its Game Creator Challenge. However, even those games that may be released under a non-free software or open source license must be made with open web technologies and Javascript as per the work criteria outlined in the announcement.

### Eich CEO promotion controversy

On March 24, 2014, Mozilla promoted [Brendan Eich](/wiki/Brendan_Eich "Brendan Eich") to the role of CEO. This led to boycotts and protests from the LGBT community and its supporters, as Eich previously donated US$1,000[^23] in 2008 in support of [California's Proposition 8](/wiki/California_Proposition_8_\(2008\) "California Proposition 8 (2008)"), a California ballot proposition and state constitutional amendment in opposition to same-sex marriage.[^24] Eich's donation first became public knowledge in 2012, while he was Mozilla’s chief technical officer, leading to angry responses on Twitter—including the use of the hashtag "#wontworkwithbigots".[^25]

Protests also emerged in 2014 following the announcement of Eich's appointment as CEO of Mozilla. U.S. companies [OkCupid](/wiki/OkCupid "OkCupid") and [CREDO Mobile](/wiki/CREDO_Mobile "CREDO Mobile") received media coverage for their objections, with the former asking its users to boycott the browser,[^26] while Credo amassed 50,000 signatures for a petition that called for Eich's resignation

Due to the controversy, Eich voluntarily stepped down on April 3, 2014[^27] and [Mitchell Baker](/wiki/Mitchell_Baker "Mitchell Baker"), executive chairwoman of Mozilla Corporation, posted a statement on the Mozilla blog: "We didn’t move fast enough to engage with people once the controversy started. Mozilla believes both in equality and freedom of speech. Equality is necessary for meaningful speech. And you need free speech to fight for equality."[^28] Eich's resignation promoted a larger backlash from conservatives who felt he had been forced out of the company internally.

OkCupid co-founder and CEO [Sam Yagan](/wiki/Sam_Yagan "Sam Yagan") had also donated $500[^29] to Republican candidate [Chris Cannon](/wiki/Chris_Cannon "Chris Cannon") who proceeded to vote for multiple measures viewed as "anti-gay", including the banning of same-sex marriage.[^30][^31][^32][^33] Yagan claims he did not know about Cannon's stance on gay rights and that his contribution was due to the candidate being the ranking Republican participating in the House subcommittee that oversaw Internet and Intellectual Property matters.[^34][^35][^36][^37][^38]

Reader comments on articles that were published close to the events were divided between support for OkCupid's actions and opposition to them. Supporters claimed the boycott was justified and saw OkCupid's actions as a firm statement of opposition to intolerance towards the gay community. Opponents saw OkCupid's actions as hypocritical, since Eich is also the inventor of [JavaScript](/wiki/Brendan_Eich#Netscape_and_JavaScript "Brendan Eich"), which is still required to browse OkCupid's website, and felt that users should not be punished for the actions of Mozilla and suspected that OkCupid's actions were a [publicity stunt](/wiki/Publicity_stunt "Publicity stunt").[^36][^39]

## Values

According to Mozilla's manifesto,[^40] which outlines goals, principles, and a pledge, "The Mozilla project uses a community-based approach to create world-class open source software and to develop new types of collaborative activities". Mozilla's manifesto mentions only its beliefs in regards to the Internet and Internet privacy, and has no mention of any political or social viewpoints.

### Pledge

According to the Mozilla Foundation:[^41]

> The Mozilla Foundation pledges to support the Mozilla Manifesto in its activities. Specifically, we will:
> 
> *   Build and enable open-source technologies and communities that support the Manifesto’s principles;
> *   Build and deliver great consumer products that support the Manifesto’s principles;
> *   Use the Mozilla assets (intellectual property such as copyrights and trademarks, infrastructure, funds, and reputation) to keep the Internet an open platform;
> *   Promote models for creating economic value for the public benefit; and
> *   Promote the Mozilla Manifesto principles in public discourse and within the Internet industry.

### Firefox

Main article: [Firefox](/wiki/Firefox "Firefox")

[Firefox](/wiki/Firefox "Firefox") is a [web browser](/wiki/Web_browser "Web browser"), and is Mozilla's [flagship](/wiki/Flagship_product "Flagship product") software product. It is available in both desktop and mobile versions. Firefox uses the [Gecko](/wiki/Gecko_\(software\) "Gecko (software)") [layout engine](/wiki/Layout_engine "Layout engine") to render web pages, which implements current and anticipated [web standards](/wiki/Web_standards "Web standards").[^42] As of late 2015, Firefox has approximately 10-11% of worldwide [usage share of web browsers](/wiki/Usage_share_of_web_browsers#Summary "Usage share of web browsers"), making it the 4th most-used web browser.[^43][^44][^45]

Firefox began as an experimental branch of the [Mozilla codebase](/wiki/Mozilla#Mozilla_Project "Mozilla") by [Dave Hyatt](/wiki/Dave_Hyatt "Dave Hyatt"), [Joe Hewitt](/wiki/Joe_Hewitt_\(programmer\) "Joe Hewitt (programmer)") and [Blake Ross](/wiki/Blake_Ross "Blake Ross"). They believed the commercial requirements of [Netscape's](/wiki/Netscape "Netscape") sponsorship and developer-driven [feature creep](/wiki/Feature_creep "Feature creep") compromised the utility of the Mozilla browser.[^46] To combat what they saw as the [Mozilla Suite's](/wiki/Mozilla_Application_Suite "Mozilla Application Suite") [software bloat](/wiki/Software_bloat "Software bloat"), they created a stand-alone browser, with which they intended to replace the Mozilla Suite.

Firefox was originally named *Phoenix* but the name was changed so as to avoid trademark conflicts with [Phoenix Technologies](/wiki/Phoenix_Technologies "Phoenix Technologies"). The initially-announced replacement, *Firebird*, provoked objections from the [Firebird](/wiki/Firebird_\(database_server\) "Firebird (database server)") project community.[^47][^48] The current name, Firefox, was chosen on February 9, 2004.[^49]

### Firefox Mobile

Main article: [Firefox Mobile](/wiki/Firefox_Mobile "Firefox Mobile")

Firefox Mobile (codenamed *Fennec*) is the build of the [Mozilla Firefox](/wiki/Firefox "Firefox") [web browser](/wiki/Web_browser "Web browser") for devices such as [smartphones](/wiki/Smartphone "Smartphone") and [tablet computers](/wiki/Tablet_computer "Tablet computer").

Firefox Mobile uses the same [Gecko](/wiki/Gecko_\(layout_engine\) "Gecko (layout engine)") [layout engine](/wiki/Layout_engine "Layout engine") as [Mozilla Firefox](/wiki/Firefox "Firefox"). For example, version 1.0 used the same engine as Firefox 3.6, and the following release, 4.0, shared core code with Firefox 4.0. Its features include [HTML5](/wiki/HTML5 "HTML5") support, [Firefox Sync](/wiki/Firefox_Sync "Firefox Sync"), [add-ons](/wiki/Add-on_\(Mozilla\) "Add-on (Mozilla)") support and [tabbed browsing](/wiki/Tabbed_browsing "Tabbed browsing").[^50]

Firefox Mobile is currently available for [Android](/wiki/Android_\(operating_system\) "Android (operating system)") 2.2 and above devices with an [ARMv7](/wiki/ARM_architecture "ARM architecture") or [ARMv6](/wiki/ARM_architecture "ARM architecture") CPU.[^51] The x86 architecture is not officially supported.[^52] [Tristan Nitot](/wiki/Tristan_Nitot "Tristan Nitot"), president of [Mozilla Europe](/wiki/Mozilla_Europe "Mozilla Europe"), has said that it's unlikely that an [iPhone](/wiki/IPhone "IPhone") or a [BlackBerry](/wiki/BlackBerry "BlackBerry") version will be released, citing [Apple's](/wiki/Apple_Inc "Apple Inc") iTunes Store application approval policies (which forbid applications competing with Apple's own, and forbid engines which run downloaded code) and BlackBerry's limited operating system as the reasons.[^53]

### Firefox OS

Main article: [Firefox OS](/wiki/Firefox_OS "Firefox OS")

Firefox OS (project name: *Boot to Gecko* also known as *B2G*) is an [open source](/wiki/Open_source "Open source") [operating system](/wiki/Operating_system "Operating system") in development by Mozilla that aims to support [HTML5](/wiki/HTML5 "HTML5") apps written using "[open Web](/wiki/Open_Web "Open Web")" technologies rather than platform-specific native [APIs](/wiki/Application_programming_interface "Application programming interface"). The concept behind Firefox OS is that all user-accessible software will be HTML5 applications, that use Open Web APIs to access the phone's hardware directly via [JavaScript](/wiki/JavaScript "JavaScript").[^54]

Some devices using this OS include[^55] Alcatel One Touch Fire, ZTE Open, LG Fireweb.

### Thunderbird

[Thunderbird](/wiki/Mozilla_Thunderbird "Mozilla Thunderbird") is a free, open source, cross-platform email and news client developed by the volunteers of the Mozilla Community.

On July 16, 2012, Mitchell Baker announced that Mozilla's leadership had come to the conclusion that on-going stability was the most important thing for Thunderbird and that innovation in Thunderbird was no longer a priority for Mozilla. In that update Baker also suggested that Mozilla had provided a pathway for community to innovate around Thunderbird if the community chooses.[^56]

### SeaMonkey

Main article: [SeaMonkey](/wiki/SeaMonkey "SeaMonkey")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0d/SeaMonkey.png)](/wiki/File:SeaMonkey.png)

SeaMonkey logo

[SeaMonkey](/wiki/SeaMonkey "SeaMonkey") (formerly the Mozilla Application Suite) is a free and open source cross platform suite of Internet software components including a web browser component, a client for sending and receiving email and [USENET](/wiki/USENET "USENET") newsgroup messages, an HTML editor ([Mozilla Composer](/wiki/Mozilla_Composer "Mozilla Composer")) and the [ChatZilla](/wiki/ChatZilla "ChatZilla") IRC client.

On March 10, 2005, [the Mozilla Foundation](/wiki/The_Mozilla_Foundation "The Mozilla Foundation") announced that it would not release any official versions of Mozilla Application Suite beyond 1.7.x, since it had now focused on the standalone applications [Firefox](/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox "Mozilla Firefox") and [Thunderbird](/wiki/Mozilla_Thunderbird "Mozilla Thunderbird").[^57] SeaMonkey is now maintained by the SeaMonkey Council, which has [trademarked](/wiki/Trademark "Trademark") the SeaMonkey name with help from the Mozilla Foundation.[^58] The Mozilla Foundation provides project hosting for the SeaMonkey developers.

### Bugzilla

Main article: [Bugzilla](/wiki/Bugzilla "Bugzilla")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Buggie.svg/220px-Buggie.svg.png)](/wiki/File:Buggie.svg)

Bugzilla logo

[Bugzilla](/wiki/Bugzilla "Bugzilla") is a [web](/wiki/World_Wide_Web "World Wide Web")-based general-purpose [bug tracking system](/wiki/Bug_tracking_system "Bug tracking system"), which was released as [open source software](/wiki/Open_source_software "Open source software") by [Netscape Communications](/wiki/Netscape_Communications "Netscape Communications") in 1998 along with the rest of the Mozilla codebase, and is currently stewarded by Mozilla. It has been adopted by a variety of organizations for use as a [bug tracking system](/wiki/Bug_tracking_system "Bug tracking system") for both [free and open source software](/wiki/Free_and_open_source_software "Free and open source software") and [proprietary](/wiki/Proprietary_software "Proprietary software") projects and products, including the [Mozilla Foundation](/wiki/The_Mozilla_Foundation "The Mozilla Foundation"), the [Linux kernel](/wiki/Linux_kernel "Linux kernel"), [GNOME](/wiki/GNOME "GNOME"), [KDE](/wiki/KDE "KDE"), [Red Hat](/wiki/Red_Hat "Red Hat"), [Novell](/wiki/Novell "Novell"), [Eclipse](/wiki/Eclipse_\(software\) "Eclipse (software)") and [LibreOffice](/wiki/LibreOffice "LibreOffice").[^59]

### Components

#### NSS

[Network Security Services](/wiki/Network_Security_Services "Network Security Services") (NSS) comprises a set of [libraries](/wiki/Library_\(computing\) "Library (computing)") designed to support [cross-platform](/wiki/Cross-platform "Cross-platform") development of security-enabled client and server applications. NSS provides a complete open-source implementation of crypto libraries supporting [SSL](/wiki/Secure_Sockets_Layer "Secure Sockets Layer") and [S/MIME](/wiki/S/MIME "S/MIME"). NSS was previously [tri-licensed](/wiki/Multi-licensing "Multi-licensing") under the [Mozilla Public License](/wiki/Mozilla_Public_License "Mozilla Public License") 1.1, the [GNU General Public License](/wiki/GNU_General_Public_License "GNU General Public License"), and the [GNU Lesser General Public License](/wiki/LGPL "LGPL"), but upgraded to GPL-compatible MPL 2.0.

[AOL](/wiki/AOL "AOL"), [Red Hat](/wiki/Red_Hat "Red Hat"), [Sun Microsystems](/wiki/Sun_Microsystems "Sun Microsystems")/[Oracle Corporation](/wiki/Oracle_Corporation "Oracle Corporation"), [Google](/wiki/Google "Google") and other companies and individual contributors have co-developed NSS and it is used in a wide range of non-Mozilla products including [Evolution](/wiki/Evolution_\(software\) "Evolution (software)"), [Pidgin](/wiki/Pidgin_\(software\) "Pidgin (software)"), and [Apache OpenOffice](/wiki/Apache_OpenOffice "Apache OpenOffice").

#### SpiderMonkey

[SpiderMonkey](/wiki/SpiderMonkey_\(software\) "SpiderMonkey (software)") is the original [JavaScript engine](/wiki/JavaScript_engine "JavaScript engine") developed by [Brendan Eich](/wiki/Brendan_Eich "Brendan Eich") when he invented [JavaScript](/wiki/JavaScript "JavaScript") in 1995 as a developer at [Netscape](/wiki/Netscape "Netscape"). It became part of the Mozilla product family when Mozilla inherited Netscape's code-base in 1998. In 2011, Eich transferred the nominal ownership of the SpiderMonkey code and project to Dave Mandelin.[^60]

SpiderMonkey is a [cross-platform](/wiki/Cross-platform "Cross-platform") engine written in [C++](/wiki/C%2B%2B "C++") which implements [ECMAScript](/wiki/ECMAScript "ECMAScript"), a standard developed from JavaScript.[^60][^61] It comprises an [interpreter](/wiki/Interpreter_\(computing\) "Interpreter (computing)"), several [just-in-time compilers](/wiki/Just-in-time_compilation "Just-in-time compilation"), a [decompiler](/wiki/Decompiler "Decompiler") and a [garbage collector](/wiki/Garbage_collection_\(computer_science\) "Garbage collection (computer science)"). Products which embed SpiderMonkey include [Firefox](/wiki/Firefox "Firefox"), [Thunderbird](/wiki/Mozilla_Thunderbird "Mozilla Thunderbird"), [SeaMonkey](/wiki/SeaMonkey "SeaMonkey"), and many non-Mozilla applications.[^62]

#### Rhino

Rhino is an [open source](/wiki/Open_source "Open source") [JavaScript engine](/wiki/JavaScript_engine "JavaScript engine") managed by the [Mozilla Foundation](/wiki/Mozilla_Foundation "Mozilla Foundation"). It is developed entirely in [Java](/wiki/Java_\(programming_language\) "Java (programming language)"). Rhino converts JavaScript scripts into Java [classes](/wiki/Class_\(computer_programming\) "Class (computer programming)"). Rhino works in both [compiled](/wiki/Compiler "Compiler") and [interpreted](/wiki/Interpreter_\(computing\) "Interpreter (computing)") mode.[^63]

#### Gecko

[Gecko](/wiki/Gecko_\(layout_engine\) "Gecko (layout engine)") is a [layout engine](/wiki/Web_browser_engine "Web browser engine") that supports web pages written using [HTML](/wiki/HTML "HTML"), [SVG](/wiki/Scalable_Vector_Graphics "Scalable Vector Graphics"), and [MathML](/wiki/MathML "MathML"). Gecko is written in [C++](/wiki/C%2B%2B "C++") and uses [NSPR](/wiki/NSPR "NSPR") for [platform independence](/wiki/Platform_independence "Platform independence"). Its source code is licensed under the [Mozilla Public License](/wiki/Mozilla_Public_License "Mozilla Public License").

Firefox uses Gecko both for rendering web pages and for rendering its [user interface](/wiki/User_interface "User interface"). Gecko is also used by Thunderbird, SeaMonkey, and many non-Mozilla applications.

#### Rust

[Rust](/wiki/Rust_\(programming_language\) "Rust (programming language)") is a compiled [programming language](/wiki/Programming_language "Programming language") being developed by Mozilla Research. It is designed for safety, concurrency, and performance. Rust is intended for creating large and complex software which needs to be both safe against exploits and fast.

Rust is being used in an experimental layout engine, [Servo](/wiki/Servo_\(layout_engine\) "Servo (layout engine)"), which is developed by Mozilla and Samsung. Servo is not used in any consumer-oriented browsers yet. However, the Servo project developers plan for parts of the Servo source code to be merged into Gecko, and Firefox, incrementally.[^64][^65]

#### XULRunner

Main article: [XULRunner](/wiki/XULRunner "XULRunner")

[XULRunner](/wiki/XULRunner "XULRunner") is a software platform and technology experiment by Mozilla, that allows applications built with the same technologies used by Firefox extensions (XPCOM, Javascript, HTML, CSS, XUL) to be run natively as desktop applications, without requiring Firefox to be installed on the user's machine. XULRunner binaries are available for the Windows, GNU/Linux and OS X operating systems, allowing such applications to be effectively cross platform.

#### pdf.js

Main article: [pdf.js](/wiki/Pdf.js "Pdf.js")

[Pdf.js](/wiki/Pdf.js "Pdf.js") is a library developed by Mozilla that allows in-browser rendering of pdf documents using the HTML5 Canvas and Javascript. It is included by default in recent versions of Firefox, allowing the browser to render pdf documents without requiring an external plugin; and it is available separately as an extension named "PDF Viewer" for Firefox for Android, SeaMonkey, and the Firefox versions which don't include it built-in. It can also be included as part of a website's scripts, to allow pdf rendering for any browser that implements the required HTML5 features and can run Javascript.

#### Shumway

[Shumway](/wiki/Shumway_\(software\) "Shumway (software)") is an open source replacement for the Adobe Flash Player, developed by Mozilla since 2012, using open web technologies as a replacement for Flash technologies. It uses Javascript and HTML5 Canvas elements to render Flash and execute Actionscript. It is included by default in Firefox Nightly and can be installed as an extension for any recent version of Firefox. The current implementation is limited in its capabilities to render Flash content outside simple projects.

## Other activities

### Mozilla VR

Mozilla VR is a team focused on bringing [Virtual reality](/wiki/Virtual_reality "Virtual reality") tools, specifications, and standards to the open Web.[^66] Mozilla VR maintains [A-Frame (VR)](/wiki/A-Frame_\(VR\) "A-Frame (VR)"), a web framework for building VR experiences, and works on advancing [WebVR](/wiki/WebVR "WebVR") support within web browsers.

### Mozilla Persona

[Mozilla Persona](/wiki/Mozilla_Persona "Mozilla Persona") is a secure, cross-browser website [authentication](/wiki/Authentication "Authentication") mechanism which allows a user to use a single username and password (or other authentication method) to log in to multiple sites.[^67] Mozilla Persona will be shutting down on November 30, 2016.[^68]

### Mozilla Location Service

This open source crowdsourced geolocation service was started by Mozilla in 2013 and offers a free [API](/wiki/Application_programming_interface "Application programming interface").

### Webmaker

Mozilla Webmaker is Mozilla's educational initiative, Webmaker's goal is to "help millions of people move from using the web to making the web." As part of Mozilla’s non-profit mission, Webmaker aims "to help the world increase their understanding of the web, take greater control of their online lives, and create a more web literate planet."[^69][^70][^70]

### Mozilla Developer Network

Mozilla maintains a comprehensive developer documentation website called the [Mozilla Developer Network](/wiki/Mozilla_Developer_Network "Mozilla Developer Network") which contains information about web technologies including [HTML](/wiki/HTML "HTML"), [CSS](/wiki/CSS "CSS"), [SVG](/wiki/SVG "SVG"), [JavaScript](/wiki/JavaScript "JavaScript"), as well Mozilla-specific information. In addition, Mozilla publishes a large number of videos about web technologies and the development of Mozilla projects on the Air Mozilla website.[^71][^72]

## Community

The Mozilla Community consists of over 40,000 active contributors from across the globe. It includes both paid employees and volunteers who work towards the goals set forth[^40] in the Mozilla Manifesto. Many of the sub-communities in Mozilla have formed around localization efforts for Mozilla Firefox, and the Mozilla web properties.

### Local communities

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/London_Mozilla_Workspace.jpg/220px-London_Mozilla_Workspace.jpg)](/wiki/File:London_Mozilla_Workspace.jpg)

Mozilla spaces, London

There are a number of sub-communities that exist based on their geographical locations, where contributors near each other work together on particular activities, such as localization, marketing, PR and user support.

### Mozilla Reps

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Mozilla_Reps.png/220px-Mozilla_Reps.png)](/wiki/File:Mozilla_Reps.png)

Mozilla Reps logo

The Mozilla Reps program aims to empower and support volunteer Mozillians who want to become official representatives of Mozilla in their region/locale.

The program provides a simple framework and a specific set of tools to help Mozillians to organize and/or attend events, recruit and mentor new contributors, document and share activities, and support their local communities better.

When joining the program, a Mozilla Rep agrees to take on the following responsibilities:

*   Represent Mozilla in their country/region
*   Promote the Mozilla Project and its mission
*   Build on and support existing/future local community efforts and programs
*   Inspire, recruit and support new contributors
*   Support and mentor future Mozilla Reps
*   Document clearly all their activities

### Conferences and events

#### Mozilla Festival

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Fireside_Chat%2C_Knight%27s_Michael_Maness_and_Dan_Sinker_-_Flickr_-_Knight_Foundation.jpg/220px-Fireside_Chat%2C_Knight%27s_Michael_Maness_and_Dan_Sinker_-_Flickr_-_Knight_Foundation.jpg)](/wiki/File:Fireside_Chat,_Knight%27s_Michael_Maness_and_Dan_Sinker_-_Flickr_-_Knight_Foundation.jpg)

Speakers from the [Knight Foundation](/wiki/Knight_Foundation "Knight Foundation") discuss the future of news at the 2011 Mozilla Festival in London.

The Mozilla Festival is an annual event where hundreds of passionate people explore the Web, learn together and make things that can change the world. With the emphasis on *making*—the mantra of the Festival is "less yack, more hack." Journalists, coders, filmmakers, designers, educators, gamers, makers, youth and anyone else, from all over the world, are encouraged to attend, with attendees from more than 40 countries, working together at the intersection between freedom, the Web, and that years theme.

The event revolves around design challenges which address key issues based on the chosen theme for that years festival. In previous years the Mozilla Festival has focused on Learning, and Media, with the 2012 festival being based around making. The titles of the festival revolve around the main theme, freedom (as in freedom of speech not free beer), and the Web.

#### MozCamps

MozCamps are the critical part of the Grow Mozilla initiative which aims to grow the Mozilla Community. These camps aim to bring core contributors from around the world together. They are intensive multi-day summits that include keynote speeches by Mozilla leadership, workshops and breakout sessions (led by paid and unpaid staff), and fun social outings. All of these activities combine to reward contributors for their hard work, engage them with new products and initiatives, and align all attendees on Mozilla's mission.

#### Mozilla Summit

Mozilla Summit are the global event with active contributors and Mozilla employees to develop a shared understanding of Mozilla's mission together. Over 2,000 people representing 90 countries and 114 languages gathered in Santa Clara, Toronto and Brussels in 2013. Mozilla has since its last summit in 2013 replaced summits with all-hands where both employees and volunteers come together to collaborate the event is a scaled down version of Mozilla Summit.
