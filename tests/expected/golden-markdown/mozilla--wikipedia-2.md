# New Zealand

[Coordinates](https://example.com/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system"): [42°S 174°E﻿ / ﻿42°S 174°E](//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=New_Zealand&params=42_S_174_E_scale:5000000_source:GNS) 

**New Zealand** ([Māori](https://example.com/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language "Māori language"): *[Aotearoa](https://example.com/wiki/Aotearoa "Aotearoa")* [\[aɔˈtɛaɾɔa\]](https://example.com/wiki/Help:IPA/M%C4%81ori "Help:IPA/Māori")) is a [sovereign](https://example.com/wiki/Sovereign_state "Sovereign state") [island country](https://example.com/wiki/Island_country "Island country") in the southwestern [Pacific Ocean](https://example.com/wiki/Pacific_Ocean "Pacific Ocean"). The country geographically comprises two main landmasses—the [North Island](https://example.com/wiki/North_Island "North Island") (*Te Ika-a-Māui*), and the [South Island](https://example.com/wiki/South_Island "South Island") (*Te Waipounamu*)—and around 600 [smaller islands](https://example.com/wiki/List_of_islands_of_New_Zealand "List of islands of New Zealand"). It has a total land area of 268,000 square kilometres (103,500 sq mi). New Zealand is situated some 2,000 kilometres (1,200 mi) east of [Australia](https://example.com/wiki/Australia "Australia") across the [Tasman Sea](https://example.com/wiki/Tasman_Sea "Tasman Sea") and roughly 1,000 kilometres (600 mi) south of the [Pacific island areas](https://example.com/wiki/List_of_islands_in_the_Pacific_Ocean "List of islands in the Pacific Ocean") of [New Caledonia](https://example.com/wiki/New_Caledonia "New Caledonia"), [Fiji](https://example.com/wiki/Fiji "Fiji"), and [Tonga](https://example.com/wiki/Tonga "Tonga"). Because of its remoteness, it was one of the last lands to be settled by humans. During its long period of isolation, New Zealand developed a distinct [biodiversity](https://example.com/wiki/Biodiversity_of_New_Zealand "Biodiversity of New Zealand") of animal, fungal, and plant life. The country's varied topography and its sharp mountain peaks, such as the [Southern Alps](https://example.com/wiki/Southern_Alps "Southern Alps"), owe much to the [tectonic uplift](https://example.com/wiki/Tectonic_uplift "Tectonic uplift") of land and volcanic eruptions. New Zealand's [capital city](https://example.com/wiki/Capital_of_New_Zealand "Capital of New Zealand") is [Wellington](https://example.com/wiki/Wellington "Wellington"), while its most populous city is [Auckland](https://example.com/wiki/Auckland "Auckland"). 

Sometime between 1250 and 1300, [Polynesians](https://example.com/wiki/Polynesians "Polynesians") settled in the islands that later were named New Zealand and developed a distinctive [Māori culture](https://example.com/wiki/M%C4%81ori_culture "Māori culture"). In 1642, Dutch explorer [Abel Tasman](https://example.com/wiki/Abel_Tasman "Abel Tasman") became the first European to sight New Zealand. In 1840, representatives of the United Kingdom and [Māori](https://example.com/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people "Māori people") chiefs signed the [Treaty of Waitangi](https://example.com/wiki/Treaty_of_Waitangi "Treaty of Waitangi"), which declared British sovereignty over the islands. In 1841, New Zealand [became a colony](https://example.com/wiki/Colony_of_New_Zealand "Colony of New Zealand") within the [British Empire](https://example.com/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") and in 1907 it [became a dominion](https://example.com/wiki/Dominion_of_New_Zealand "Dominion of New Zealand"); it gained [full statutory independence](https://example.com/wiki/Independence_of_New_Zealand "Independence of New Zealand") in 1947 and the British monarch remained the [head of state](https://example.com/wiki/Head_of_state "Head of state"). Today, the majority of [New Zealand's population](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand%27s_population "New Zealand's population") of 4.9 million is of [European descent](https://example.com/wiki/European_New_Zealanders "European New Zealanders"); the indigenous Māori are the largest minority, followed by [Asians](https://example.com/wiki/Asian_New_Zealanders "Asian New Zealanders") and [Pacific Islanders](https://example.com/wiki/Pacific_Islander "Pacific Islander"). Reflecting this, [New Zealand's culture](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand%27s_culture "New Zealand's culture") is mainly derived from Māori and early British settlers, with recent broadening arising from increased [immigration](https://example.com/wiki/Immigration_to_New_Zealand "Immigration to New Zealand"). The [official languages](https://example.com/wiki/Languages_of_New_Zealand "Languages of New Zealand") are [English](https://example.com/wiki/English_language "English language"), [Māori](https://example.com/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language "Māori language"), and [New Zealand Sign Language](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Sign_Language "New Zealand Sign Language"), with English being very dominant. 

A [developed country](https://example.com/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country"), New Zealand [ranks highly](https://example.com/wiki/International_rankings_of_New_Zealand "International rankings of New Zealand") in international comparisons of national performance, such as quality of life, health, education, protection of [civil liberties](https://example.com/wiki/Civil_liberties "Civil liberties"), and [economic freedom](https://example.com/wiki/Economic_freedom "Economic freedom"). New Zealand underwent [major economic changes](https://example.com/wiki/Rogernomics "Rogernomics") during the 1980s, which transformed it from a [protectionist](https://example.com/wiki/Protectionist "Protectionist") to a [liberalised](https://example.com/wiki/Economic_liberalization "Economic liberalization") [free-trade](https://example.com/wiki/Free-trade "Free-trade") economy. The service sector dominates the [national economy](https://example.com/wiki/Economy_of_New_Zealand "Economy of New Zealand"), followed by the industrial sector, and [agriculture](https://example.com/wiki/Agriculture_in_New_Zealand "Agriculture in New Zealand"); international [tourism](https://example.com/wiki/Tourism_in_New_Zealand "Tourism in New Zealand") is a significant source of revenue. Nationally, legislative authority is vested in an elected, [unicameral](https://example.com/wiki/Unicameral "Unicameral") [Parliament](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Parliament "New Zealand Parliament"), while executive political power is exercised by the [Cabinet](https://example.com/wiki/Cabinet_of_New_Zealand "Cabinet of New Zealand"), led by the [prime minister](https://example.com/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand "Prime Minister of New Zealand"), currently [Jacinda Ardern](https://example.com/wiki/Jacinda_Ardern "Jacinda Ardern"). [Queen Elizabeth II](https://example.com/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II "Queen Elizabeth II") is the [country's monarch](https://example.com/wiki/Monarchy_of_New_Zealand "Monarchy of New Zealand") and is represented by a [governor-general](https://example.com/wiki/Governor-General_of_New_Zealand "Governor-General of New Zealand"), currently [Dame Patsy Reddy](https://example.com/wiki/Dame_Patsy_Reddy "Dame Patsy Reddy"). In addition, New Zealand is organised into 11 [regional councils](https://example.com/wiki/Regions_of_New_Zealand "Regions of New Zealand") and 67 [territorial authorities](https://example.com/wiki/Territorial_authorities_of_New_Zealand "Territorial authorities of New Zealand") for local government purposes. The [Realm of New Zealand](https://example.com/wiki/Realm_of_New_Zealand "Realm of New Zealand") also includes [Tokelau](https://example.com/wiki/Tokelau "Tokelau") (a [dependent territory](https://example.com/wiki/Dependent_territory "Dependent territory")); the [Cook Islands](https://example.com/wiki/Cook_Islands "Cook Islands") and [Niue](https://example.com/wiki/Niue "Niue") (self-governing states in [free association](https://example.com/wiki/Associated_state "Associated state") with New Zealand); and the [Ross Dependency](https://example.com/wiki/Ross_Dependency "Ross Dependency"), which is New Zealand's [territorial claim in Antarctica](https://example.com/wiki/Territorial_claim_in_Antarctica "Territorial claim in Antarctica"). New Zealand is a member of the [United Nations](https://example.com/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations"), [Commonwealth of Nations](https://example.com/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations"), [ANZUS](https://example.com/wiki/ANZUS "ANZUS"), [Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development](https://example.com/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development "Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development"), [ASEAN Plus Six](https://example.com/wiki/Association_of_Southeast_Asian_Nations#ASEAN_Plus_Three_and_Six "Association of Southeast Asian Nations"), [Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation](https://example.com/wiki/Asia-Pacific_Economic_Cooperation "Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation"), the [Pacific Community](https://example.com/wiki/Pacific_Community "Pacific Community") and the [Pacific Islands Forum](https://example.com/wiki/Pacific_Islands_Forum "Pacific Islands Forum"). 

## Etymology

[![Brown square paper with Dutch writing and a thick red, curved line](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Detail_of_1657_map_Polus_Antarcticus_by_Jan_Janssonius%2C_showing_Nova_Zeelandia.png/220px-Detail_of_1657_map_Polus_Antarcticus_by_Jan_Janssonius%2C_showing_Nova_Zeelandia.png)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Detail_of_1657_map_Polus_Antarcticus_by_Jan_Janssonius,_showing_Nova_Zeelandia.png)

Detail from a 1657 map showing the western coastline of "Nova Zeelandia". (In this map, north is at the bottom.) 

[Dutch](https://example.com/wiki/Netherlands "Netherlands") explorer Abel Tasman sighted New Zealand in 1642 and named it *Staten Land* "in honour of the [States General](https://example.com/wiki/States_General_of_the_Netherlands "States General of the Netherlands")" (Dutch parliament). He wrote, "it is possible that this land joins to the Staten Land but it is uncertain",[^22] referring to [a landmass of the same name](https://example.com/wiki/Isla_de_los_Estados "Isla de los Estados") at the southern tip of South America, discovered by [Jacob Le Maire](https://example.com/wiki/Jacob_Le_Maire "Jacob Le Maire") in 1616.[^23][^24] In 1645, Dutch [cartographers](https://example.com/wiki/Cartographers "Cartographers") renamed the land *Nova Zeelandia* after the [Dutch province](https://example.com/wiki/Seventeen_Provinces "Seventeen Provinces") of [Zeeland](https://example.com/wiki/Zeeland "Zeeland").[^25][^26] British explorer [James Cook](https://example.com/wiki/James_Cook "James Cook") subsequently [anglicised](https://example.com/wiki/Anglicisation "Anglicisation") the name to New Zealand.[^27] 

*[Aotearoa](https://example.com/wiki/Aotearoa "Aotearoa")* (pronounced [/ˌaʊtɛəˈroʊ.ə/](https://example.com/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English"); often translated as "land of the long white cloud")[^28] is the current Māori name for New Zealand. It is unknown whether Māori had a name for the whole country before the arrival of Europeans, with *Aotearoa* originally referring to just the [North Island](https://example.com/wiki/North_Island "North Island").[^29] Māori had several traditional names for the two main islands, including *Te Ika-a-Māui* (the fish of [Māui](https://example.com/wiki/M%C4%81ui_\(M%C4%81ori_mythology\) "Māui (Māori mythology)")) for the North Island and *Te Waipounamu* (the waters of [greenstone](https://example.com/wiki/Pounamu "Pounamu")) or *Te Waka o Aoraki* (the canoe of [Aoraki](https://example.com/wiki/Aoraki "Aoraki")) for the [South Island](https://example.com/wiki/South_Island "South Island").[^30] Early European maps labelled the islands North (North Island), Middle (South Island) and South ([Stewart Island / Rakiura](https://example.com/wiki/Stewart_Island_/_Rakiura "Stewart Island / Rakiura")).[^31] In 1830, mapmakers began to use "North" and "South" on their maps to distinguish the two largest islands and by 1907 this was the accepted norm.[^27] The [New Zealand Geographic Board](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Geographic_Board "New Zealand Geographic Board") discovered in 2009 that the names of the North Island and South Island had never been formalised, and names and alternative names were formalised in 2013. This set the names as North Island or Te Ika-a-Māui, and South Island or Te Waipounamu.[^32] For each island, either its English or Māori name can be used, or both can be used together.[^32] 

## History

[![One set of arrows point from Taiwan to Melanesia to Fiji/Samoa and then to the Marquesas Islands. The population then spread, some going south to New Zealand and others going north to Hawai'i. A second set start in southern Asia and end in Melanesia.](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Polynesian_Migration.svg/290px-Polynesian_Migration.svg.png)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Polynesian_Migration.svg)

The [Māori people](https://example.com/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people "Māori people") are most likely descended from people who emigrated from [Taiwan](https://example.com/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") to [Melanesia](https://example.com/wiki/Melanesia "Melanesia") and then travelled east through to the [Society Islands](https://example.com/wiki/Society_Islands "Society Islands"). After a pause of 70 to 265 years, a new wave of exploration led to the discovery and settlement of New Zealand.[^33] 

New Zealand was one of the last major landmasses settled by humans. [Radiocarbon dating](https://example.com/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating "Radiocarbon dating"), evidence of [deforestation](https://example.com/wiki/Deforestation "Deforestation")[^34] and [mitochondrial DNA](https://example.com/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA "Mitochondrial DNA") variability within [Māori](https://example.com/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people "Māori people") populations[^35] suggest New Zealand was first settled by Eastern [Polynesians](https://example.com/wiki/Polynesians "Polynesians") between 1250 and 1300,[^30][^36] concluding a long series of voyages through the southern Pacific islands.[^37] Over the centuries that followed, these settlers developed a distinct culture now known as Māori. The population was divided into *[iwi](https://example.com/wiki/Iwi "Iwi")* (tribes) and *[hapū](https://example.com/wiki/Hap%C5%AB "Hapū")* (subtribes) who would sometimes cooperate, sometimes compete and sometimes fight against each other.[^38] At some point a group of Māori migrated to *Rēkohu*, now known as the [Chatham Islands](https://example.com/wiki/Chatham_Islands "Chatham Islands"), where they developed their distinct [Moriori](https://example.com/wiki/Moriori "Moriori") culture.[^39][^40] The Moriori population was all but wiped out between 1835 and 1862, largely because of [Taranaki](https://example.com/wiki/Taranaki_\(iwi\) "Taranaki (iwi)") Māori invasion and enslavement in the 1830s, although European diseases also contributed. In 1862 only 101 survived, and the last known full-blooded Moriori died in 1933.[^41] 

[![An engraving of a sketched coastline on white background](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg/170px-Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg)

Map of the New Zealand coastline as Cook charted it on his [first visit](https://example.com/wiki/First_voyage_of_James_Cook "First voyage of James Cook") in 1769–70. The track of the *[Endeavour](https://example.com/wiki/HMS_Endeavour "HMS Endeavour")* is also shown. 

The first [Europeans](https://example.com/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Europe "Ethnic groups in Europe") known to have reached New Zealand were Dutch explorer [Abel Tasman](https://example.com/wiki/Abel_Tasman "Abel Tasman") and his crew in 1642.[^42] In a hostile encounter, four crew members were killed and at least one Māori was hit by [canister shot](https://example.com/wiki/Canister_shot "Canister shot").[^43] Europeans did not revisit New Zealand until 1769 when British explorer [James Cook](https://example.com/wiki/James_Cook "James Cook") mapped almost the entire coastline.[^42] Following Cook, New Zealand was visited by numerous European and North American [whaling](https://example.com/wiki/History_of_whaling "History of whaling"), [sealing](https://example.com/wiki/Seal_hunting "Seal hunting") and trading ships. They traded European food, metal tools, weapons and other goods for timber, Māori food, artefacts and water.[^44] The introduction of the potato and the [musket](https://example.com/wiki/Musket "Musket") transformed Māori agriculture and warfare. Potatoes provided a reliable food surplus, which enabled longer and more sustained military campaigns.[^45] The resulting intertribal [Musket Wars](https://example.com/wiki/Musket_Wars "Musket Wars") encompassed over 600 battles between 1801 and 1840, killing 30,000–40,000 Māori.[^46] From the early 19th century, Christian [missionaries](https://example.com/wiki/Missionary "Missionary") began to settle New Zealand, eventually [converting](https://example.com/wiki/Religious_conversion "Religious conversion") most of the Māori population.[^47] The Māori population declined to around 40% of its pre-contact level during the 19th century; introduced diseases were the major factor.[^48] 

[![A torn sheet of paper](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Treatyofwaitangi.jpg/170px-Treatyofwaitangi.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Treatyofwaitangi.jpg)

The Waitangi sheet from the [Treaty of Waitangi](https://example.com/wiki/Treaty_of_Waitangi "Treaty of Waitangi") 

In 1788 Captain [Arthur Phillip](https://example.com/wiki/Arthur_Phillip "Arthur Phillip") assumed the position of [Governor](https://example.com/wiki/Governor_of_New_South_Wales "Governor of New South Wales") of the new British colony of [New South Wales](https://example.com/wiki/Colony_of_New_South_Wales "Colony of New South Wales") which according to his commission included New Zealand.[^49] The British Government appointed [James Busby](https://example.com/wiki/James_Busby "James Busby") as British Resident to New Zealand in 1832 following a petition from northern Māori.[^50] In 1835, following an announcement of impending French settlement by [Charles de Thierry](https://example.com/wiki/Charles_de_Thierry "Charles de Thierry"), the nebulous [United Tribes of New Zealand](https://example.com/wiki/United_Tribes_of_New_Zealand "United Tribes of New Zealand") sent a [Declaration of Independence](https://example.com/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Independence_of_New_Zealand "Declaration of the Independence of New Zealand") to King [William IV of the United Kingdom](https://example.com/wiki/William_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom "William IV of the United Kingdom") asking for protection.[^50] Ongoing unrest, the proposed settlement of New Zealand by the [New Zealand Company](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Company "New Zealand Company") (which had already sent its first ship of surveyors to buy land from Māori) and the dubious legal standing of the Declaration of Independence prompted the [Colonial Office](https://example.com/wiki/Colonial_Office "Colonial Office") to send Captain [William Hobson](https://example.com/wiki/William_Hobson "William Hobson") to claim sovereignty for the [United Kingdom](https://example.com/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") and negotiate a treaty with the Māori.[^51] The [Treaty of Waitangi](https://example.com/wiki/Treaty_of_Waitangi "Treaty of Waitangi") was first signed in the [Bay of Islands](https://example.com/wiki/Bay_of_Islands "Bay of Islands") on 6 February 1840.[^52] In response to the New Zealand Company's attempts to establish an independent settlement in [Wellington](https://example.com/wiki/Wellington "Wellington")[^53] and French settlers purchasing land in [Akaroa](https://example.com/wiki/Akaroa "Akaroa"),[^54] Hobson declared British sovereignty over all of New Zealand on 21 May 1840, even though copies of the Treaty were still circulating throughout the country for Māori to sign.[^55] With the signing of the Treaty and declaration of sovereignty the number of immigrants, particularly from the United Kingdom, began to increase.[^56] 

[![Black and white engraving depicting a crowd of people](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/1863_Meeting_of_Settlers_and_Maoris_at_Hawke%27s_Bay%2C_New_Zealand.jpg/290px-1863_Meeting_of_Settlers_and_Maoris_at_Hawke%27s_Bay%2C_New_Zealand.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:1863_Meeting_of_Settlers_and_Maoris_at_Hawke%27s_Bay,_New_Zealand.jpg)

A meeting of European and Māori inhabitants of [Hawke's Bay Province](https://example.com/wiki/Hawke%27s_Bay_Province "Hawke's Bay Province"). Engraving, 1863. 

New Zealand, still part of the colony of New South Wales, became a separate [Colony](https://example.com/wiki/Crown_colony "Crown colony") [of New Zealand](https://example.com/wiki/Colony_of_New_Zealand "Colony of New Zealand") on 1 July 1841.[^57] Armed conflict began between the Colonial government and Māori in 1843 with the [Wairau Affray](https://example.com/wiki/Wairau_Affray "Wairau Affray") over land and disagreements over sovereignty. These conflicts, mainly in the North Island, saw thousands of Imperial troops and the Royal Navy come to New Zealand and became known as the [New Zealand Wars](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Wars "New Zealand Wars"). Following these armed conflicts, large amounts of [Māori land was confiscated by the government](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_land_confiscations "New Zealand land confiscations") to meet settler demands.[^58] 

The colony gained a [representative government in 1852](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Constitution_Act_1852 "New Zealand Constitution Act 1852") and the [first Parliament](https://example.com/wiki/1st_New_Zealand_Parliament "1st New Zealand Parliament") met in 1854.[^59] In 1856 the colony effectively became self-governing, gaining responsibility over all domestic matters other than [native](https://example.com/wiki/Indigenous_peoples "Indigenous peoples") policy.[^59] (Control over native policy was granted in the mid-1860s.[^59]) Following concerns that the South Island might form a separate colony, premier [Alfred Domett](https://example.com/wiki/Alfred_Domett "Alfred Domett") moved a resolution to transfer the [capital](https://example.com/wiki/Capital_of_New_Zealand "Capital of New Zealand") from Auckland to a locality near [Cook Strait](https://example.com/wiki/Cook_Strait "Cook Strait").[^60] Wellington was chosen for its central location, with Parliament officially sitting there for the first time in 1865.[^61] 

In 1891 the [Liberal Party](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Liberal_Party "New Zealand Liberal Party") came to power as the first organised political party.[^62] The [Liberal Government](https://example.com/wiki/Liberal_Government_of_New_Zealand "Liberal Government of New Zealand"), led by [Richard Seddon](https://example.com/wiki/Richard_Seddon "Richard Seddon") for most of its period in office,[^63] passed many important social and economic measures. In 1893 New Zealand was the first nation in the world to grant all [women the right to vote](https://example.com/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage_in_New_Zealand "Women's suffrage in New Zealand")[^62] and in 1894 pioneered the [adoption of compulsory arbitration between employers and unions](https://example.com/wiki/Industrial_Conciliation_and_Arbitration_Act_1894 "Industrial Conciliation and Arbitration Act 1894").[^64] 

In 1907, at the request of the New Zealand Parliament, King [Edward VII](https://example.com/wiki/Edward_VII "Edward VII") proclaimed New Zealand a [Dominion](https://example.com/wiki/Dominion_of_New_Zealand "Dominion of New Zealand") within the British Empire,[^65] reflecting its self-governing status.[^66] In 1947 the country [adopted](https://example.com/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_Adoption_Act_1947 "Statute of Westminster Adoption Act 1947") the [Statute of Westminster](https://example.com/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931 "Statute of Westminster 1931"), confirming that the British Parliament could no longer legislate for New Zealand without the consent of New Zealand.[^59] 

Early in the 20th century, New Zealand was involved in world affairs, fighting in the [First](https://example.com/wiki/Military_history_of_New_Zealand_in_World_War_I "Military history of New Zealand in World War I") and [Second World Wars](https://example.com/wiki/Military_history_of_New_Zealand_during_World_War_II "Military history of New Zealand during World War II")[^67] and suffering through the [Great Depression](https://example.com/wiki/Great_Depression "Great Depression").[^68] The depression led to the election of the [First Labour Government](https://example.com/wiki/First_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand "First Labour Government of New Zealand") and the establishment of a comprehensive [welfare state](https://example.com/wiki/Welfare_state "Welfare state") and a [protectionist](https://example.com/wiki/Protectionist "Protectionist") economy.[^69] New Zealand experienced increasing prosperity following the Second World War[^70] and Māori began to leave their traditional rural life and move to the cities in search of work.[^71] A [Māori protest movement](https://example.com/wiki/M%C4%81ori_protest_movement "Māori protest movement") developed, which criticised [Eurocentrism](https://example.com/wiki/Eurocentrism "Eurocentrism") and worked for greater recognition of [Māori culture](https://example.com/wiki/M%C4%81ori_culture "Māori culture") and of the Treaty of Waitangi.[^72] In 1975, a [Waitangi Tribunal](https://example.com/wiki/Waitangi_Tribunal "Waitangi Tribunal") was set up to investigate alleged breaches of the Treaty, and it was enabled to investigate historic grievances in 1985.[^52] The government has negotiated [settlements of these grievances](https://example.com/wiki/Treaty_of_Waitangi_claims_and_settlements "Treaty of Waitangi claims and settlements") with many iwi,[^73] although [Māori claims to the foreshore and seabed](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_foreshore_and_seabed_controversy "New Zealand foreshore and seabed controversy") have proved controversial in the 2000s.[^74][^75]

New Zealand is a [constitutional monarchy](https://example.com/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy "Constitutional monarchy") with a [parliamentary democracy](https://example.com/wiki/Parliamentary_democracy "Parliamentary democracy"),[^76] although [its constitution](https://example.com/wiki/Constitution_of_New_Zealand "Constitution of New Zealand") is [not codified](https://example.com/wiki/Uncodified_constitution "Uncodified constitution").[^77] [Elizabeth II](https://example.com/wiki/Elizabeth_II "Elizabeth II") is the [Queen of New Zealand](https://example.com/wiki/Queen_of_New_Zealand "Queen of New Zealand")[^78] and thus the [head of state](https://example.com/wiki/Head_of_state "Head of state").[^79] The Queen is represented by the [governor-general](https://example.com/wiki/Governor-General_of_New_Zealand "Governor-General of New Zealand"), whom she appoints on the [advice](https://example.com/wiki/Advice_\(constitutional\) "Advice (constitutional)") of the [prime minister](https://example.com/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand "Prime Minister of New Zealand").[^80] The governor-general can exercise the Crown's [prerogative powers](https://example.com/wiki/Prerogative_powers "Prerogative powers"), such as reviewing cases of injustice and making appointments of [ministers](https://example.com/wiki/Ministers_of_the_New_Zealand_Government "Ministers of the New Zealand Government"), ambassadors and other key public officials,[^81] and in rare situations, the [reserve powers](https://example.com/wiki/Reserve_power "Reserve power") (e.g. the power to dissolve parliament or refuse the [royal assent](https://example.com/wiki/Royal_assent "Royal assent") of a [bill](https://example.com/wiki/Bill_\(law\) "Bill (law)") into law).[^82] The powers of the monarch and the governor-general are limited by constitutional constraints and they cannot normally be exercised without the advice of ministers.[^82] 

The [New Zealand Parliament](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Parliament "New Zealand Parliament") holds [legislative power](https://example.com/wiki/Legislative_power "Legislative power") and consists of the Queen and the [House of Representatives](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_House_of_Representatives "New Zealand House of Representatives").[^83] It also included an upper house, the [Legislative Council](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Legislative_Council "New Zealand Legislative Council"), until this was abolished in 1950.[^83] The [supremacy of parliament](https://example.com/wiki/Supremacy_of_parliament "Supremacy of parliament") over the Crown and other government institutions was established in England by the [Bill of Rights 1689](https://example.com/wiki/Bill_of_Rights_1689 "Bill of Rights 1689") and has been ratified as law in New Zealand.[^83] The House of Representatives is democratically elected and a government is formed from the party or [coalition](https://example.com/wiki/Coalition_government "Coalition government") with the majority of seats. If no majority is formed, a [minority government](https://example.com/wiki/Minority_government "Minority government") can be formed if support from other parties during [confidence and supply](https://example.com/wiki/Confidence_and_supply "Confidence and supply") votes is assured.[^83] The governor-general appoints ministers under advice from the prime minister, who is by [convention](https://example.com/wiki/Constitutional_convention_\(political_custom\) "Constitutional convention (political custom)") the [parliamentary leader](https://example.com/wiki/Parliamentary_leader "Parliamentary leader") of the governing party or coalition.[^84] [Cabinet](https://example.com/wiki/Cabinet_of_New_Zealand "Cabinet of New Zealand"), formed by ministers and led by the prime minister, is the highest policy-making body in government and responsible for deciding significant government actions.[^85] Members of Cabinet make major decisions collectively, and are therefore [collectively responsible](https://example.com/wiki/Cabinet_collective_responsibility "Cabinet collective responsibility") for the consequences of these decisions.[^86] 

A [parliamentary general election](https://example.com/wiki/Elections_in_New_Zealand "Elections in New Zealand") must be called no later than three years after the previous election.[^87] Almost all general elections between [1853](https://example.com/wiki/1853_New_Zealand_general_election "1853 New Zealand general election") and [1993](https://example.com/wiki/1993_New_Zealand_general_election "1993 New Zealand general election") were held under the [first-past-the-post voting](https://example.com/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting "First-past-the-post voting") system.[^88] Since the [1996 election](https://example.com/wiki/1996_New_Zealand_general_election "1996 New Zealand general election"), a form of [proportional representation](https://example.com/wiki/Proportional_representation "Proportional representation") called [mixed-member proportional](https://example.com/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional "Mixed-member proportional") (MMP) has been used.[^77] Under the MMP system, each person has two votes; one is for a candidate standing in the voter's [electorate](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_electorates "New Zealand electorates") and the other is for a party. Since the [2014 election](https://example.com/wiki/2014_New_Zealand_general_election "2014 New Zealand general election"), there have been 71 electorates (which include seven [Māori electorates](https://example.com/wiki/M%C4%81ori_electorates "Māori electorates") in which only Māori can optionally vote),[^89] and the remaining 49 of the 120 seats are assigned so that representation in parliament reflects the party vote, with the threshold that a party must win at least one electorate or 5% of the total party vote before it is eligible for a seat.[^90] 

[![A block of buildings fronted by a large statue.](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Seddon_Statue_in_Parliament_Grounds.jpg/220px-Seddon_Statue_in_Parliament_Grounds.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Seddon_Statue_in_Parliament_Grounds.jpg)

A statue of [Richard Seddon](https://example.com/wiki/Richard_Seddon "Richard Seddon"), the "[Beehive](https://example.com/wiki/Beehive_\(New_Zealand\) "Beehive (New Zealand)")" (Executive Wing), and [Parliament House](https://example.com/wiki/Parliament_House,_Wellington "Parliament House, Wellington") (right), in Parliament Grounds, [Wellington](https://example.com/wiki/Wellington "Wellington"). 

Elections since the 1930s have been dominated by two political parties, [National](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party "New Zealand National Party") and [Labour](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party "New Zealand Labour Party").[^88] Between March 2005 and August 2006, New Zealand became the first country in the world in which all the highest offices in the land—head of state, governor-general, prime minister, [speaker](https://example.com/wiki/Speaker_of_the_New_Zealand_House_of_Representatives "Speaker of the New Zealand House of Representatives") and [chief justice](https://example.com/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_New_Zealand "Chief Justice of New Zealand")—were occupied simultaneously by women.[^91] The current prime minister is [Jacinda Ardern](https://example.com/wiki/Jacinda_Ardern "Jacinda Ardern"), who has been in office since 26 October 2017.[^92] She is the country's third female prime minister.[^93] 

[New Zealand's judiciary](https://example.com/wiki/Judiciary_of_New_Zealand "Judiciary of New Zealand"), headed by the chief justice,[^94] includes the [Supreme Court](https://example.com/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_New_Zealand "Supreme Court of New Zealand"), [Court of Appeal](https://example.com/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_New_Zealand "Court of Appeal of New Zealand"), the [High Court](https://example.com/wiki/High_Court_of_New_Zealand "High Court of New Zealand"), and subordinate courts.[^95] Judges and judicial officers are appointed non-politically and under strict rules regarding tenure to help maintain [judicial independence](https://example.com/wiki/Judicial_independence "Judicial independence").[^77] This theoretically allows the judiciary to interpret the law based solely on the legislation enacted by Parliament without other influences on their decisions.[^96] 

New Zealand is identified as one of the world's most stable and well-governed states.[^97] As at 2017, the country was ranked fourth in the strength of its democratic institutions,[^98] and first in government transparency and [lack of corruption](https://example.com/wiki/Corruption_in_New_Zealand "Corruption in New Zealand").[^99] A 2017 [Human Rights Report](https://example.com/wiki/Country_Reports_on_Human_Rights_Practices "Country Reports on Human Rights Practices") by the U.S. Department of State noted that the government generally [respected the rights](https://example.com/wiki/Human_rights_in_New_Zealand "Human rights in New Zealand") of individuals, but voiced concerns regarding the social status of the Māori population.[^100] New Zealand ranks highly for civic participation in the political process, with 77% [voter turnout](https://example.com/wiki/Voter_turnout "Voter turnout") during recent elections, compared to an OECD average of 69%.[^101] 

### Foreign relations and military

Early colonial New Zealand allowed the British Government to determine external trade and be responsible for foreign policy.[^102] The 1923 and 1926 [Imperial Conferences](https://example.com/wiki/Imperial_Conference "Imperial Conference") decided that New Zealand should be allowed to negotiate its own political [treaties](https://example.com/wiki/Treaty "Treaty") and the first commercial treaty was ratified in 1928 with Japan. On 3 September 1939 New Zealand allied itself with Britain and [declared war](https://example.com/wiki/Declaration_of_war "Declaration of war") on Germany with Prime Minister [Michael Joseph Savage](https://example.com/wiki/Michael_Joseph_Savage "Michael Joseph Savage") proclaiming, "Where she goes, we go; where she stands, we stand."[^103] 

In 1951 the United Kingdom became increasingly focused on its European interests,[^104] while New Zealand joined [Australia](https://example.com/wiki/Australia%E2%80%93New_Zealand_relations "Australia–New Zealand relations") and the [United States](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand%E2%80%93United_States_relations "New Zealand–United States relations") in the [ANZUS](https://example.com/wiki/ANZUS "ANZUS") security treaty.[^105] The influence of the United States on New Zealand weakened following protests over the [Vietnam War](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_in_the_Vietnam_War "New Zealand in the Vietnam War"),[^106] the refusal of the United States to admonish France after the [sinking of the *Rainbow Warrior*](https://example.com/wiki/Sinking_of_the_Rainbow_Warrior "Sinking of the Rainbow Warrior"),[^107] disagreements over environmental and agricultural trade issues and [New Zealand's nuclear-free policy](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_nuclear-free_zone "New Zealand nuclear-free zone").[^108][^109] Despite the United States' suspension of ANZUS obligations the treaty remained in effect between New Zealand and Australia, whose foreign policy has followed a similar historical trend.[^110] Close political contact is maintained between the two countries, with [free trade agreements](https://example.com/wiki/Closer_Economic_Relations "Closer Economic Relations") and [travel arrangements](https://example.com/wiki/Trans-Tasman_Travel_Arrangement "Trans-Tasman Travel Arrangement") that allow citizens to visit, live and work in both countries without restrictions.[^111] In 2013 there were about 650,000 New Zealand citizens living in Australia, which is equivalent to 15% of the resident population of New Zealand.[^112] 

[![A soldier in a green army uniform faces forwards](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/ANZAC_Day_service_at_the_National_War_Memorial_-_Flickr_-_NZ_Defence_Force_%2820%29.jpg/220px-ANZAC_Day_service_at_the_National_War_Memorial_-_Flickr_-_NZ_Defence_Force_%2820%29.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:ANZAC_Day_service_at_the_National_War_Memorial_-_Flickr_-_NZ_Defence_Force_\(20\).jpg)

[Anzac Day](https://example.com/wiki/Anzac_Day "Anzac Day") service at the National War Memorial 

New Zealand has a strong presence among the [Pacific Island](https://example.com/wiki/Pacific_Island "Pacific Island") countries. A large proportion of New Zealand's aid goes to these countries and many Pacific people migrate to New Zealand for employment.[^113] Permanent migration is regulated under the 1970 Samoan Quota Scheme and the 2002 Pacific Access Category, which allow up to 1,100 Samoan nationals and up to 750 other Pacific Islanders respectively to become permanent New Zealand residents each year. A seasonal workers scheme for temporary migration was introduced in 2007 and in 2009 about 8,000 Pacific Islanders were employed under it.[^114] A [regional power](https://example.com/wiki/Regional_power "Regional power"),[^115] New Zealand is involved in the [Pacific Islands Forum](https://example.com/wiki/Pacific_Islands_Forum "Pacific Islands Forum"), the [Pacific Community](https://example.com/wiki/Pacific_Community "Pacific Community"), [Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation](https://example.com/wiki/Asia-Pacific_Economic_Cooperation "Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation") and the [Association of Southeast Asian Nations](https://example.com/wiki/Association_of_Southeast_Asian_Nations "Association of Southeast Asian Nations") Regional Forum (including the [East Asia Summit](https://example.com/wiki/East_Asia_Summit "East Asia Summit")).[^111] New Zealand is a member of the [United Nations](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_and_the_United_Nations "New Zealand and the United Nations"),[^116] the [Commonwealth of Nations](https://example.com/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations "Commonwealth of Nations")[^117] and the [Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development](https://example.com/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development "Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development") (OECD),[^118] and participates in the [Five Power Defence Arrangements](https://example.com/wiki/Five_Power_Defence_Arrangements "Five Power Defence Arrangements").[^119] 

New Zealand's military services—the Defence Force—comprise the [New Zealand Army](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Army "New Zealand Army"), the [Royal New Zealand Air Force](https://example.com/wiki/Royal_New_Zealand_Air_Force "Royal New Zealand Air Force") and the [Royal New Zealand Navy](https://example.com/wiki/Royal_New_Zealand_Navy "Royal New Zealand Navy").[^120] New Zealand's [national defence](https://example.com/wiki/National_security "National security") needs are modest, since a direct attack is unlikely.[^121] However, its military has [had a global presence](https://example.com/wiki/Military_history_of_New_Zealand "Military history of New Zealand"). The country fought in both world wars, with notable campaigns in [Gallipoli](https://example.com/wiki/Gallipoli_Campaign "Gallipoli Campaign"), [Crete](https://example.com/wiki/Battle_of_Crete "Battle of Crete"),[^122] [El Alamein](https://example.com/wiki/Second_Battle_of_El_Alamein "Second Battle of El Alamein")[^123] and [Cassino](https://example.com/wiki/Battle_of_Monte_Cassino "Battle of Monte Cassino").[^124] The Gallipoli campaign played an important part in fostering New Zealand's [national identity](https://example.com/wiki/National_identity "National identity")[^125][^126] and strengthened the [ANZAC](https://example.com/wiki/Australian_and_New_Zealand_Army_Corps "Australian and New Zealand Army Corps") tradition it shares with Australia.[^127] 

In addition to Vietnam and the two world wars, New Zealand fought in the [Second Boer War](https://example.com/wiki/Second_Boer_War "Second Boer War"),[^128] the [Korean War](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_in_the_Korean_War "New Zealand in the Korean War"),[^129] the [Malayan Emergency](https://example.com/wiki/Malayan_Emergency "Malayan Emergency"),[^130] the [Gulf War](https://example.com/wiki/Gulf_War "Gulf War") and the [Afghanistan War](https://example.com/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_\(2001%E2%80%93present\) "War in Afghanistan (2001–present)"). It has contributed forces to several regional and global peacekeeping missions, such as those in [Cyprus](https://example.com/wiki/Cyprus_dispute "Cyprus dispute"), [Somalia](https://example.com/wiki/Somali_Civil_War "Somali Civil War"), [Bosnia and Herzegovina](https://example.com/wiki/Bosnian_War "Bosnian War"), the [Sinai](https://example.com/wiki/Suez_Crisis "Suez Crisis"), [Angola](https://example.com/wiki/Angolan_Civil_War "Angolan Civil War"), [Cambodia](https://example.com/wiki/Cambodian%E2%80%93Vietnamese_War "Cambodian–Vietnamese War"), the [Iran–Iraq](https://example.com/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War "Iran–Iraq War") border, [Bougainville](https://example.com/wiki/Bougainville_Campaign "Bougainville Campaign"), [East Timor](https://example.com/wiki/Operation_Astute "Operation Astute"), and the [Solomon Islands](https://example.com/wiki/Solomon_Islands#Civil_war "Solomon Islands").[^131] 

### Local government and external territories

[![Map with the North, South, Stewart/Rakiura, Tokelau, Cook, Niue, Kermadec, Chatham, Bounty, Antipodes, Snare, Auckland and Campbell Islands highlighted. New Zealand's segment of Antarctica (the Ross Dependency) is also highlighted.](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/NZL_orthographic_NaturalEarth_labelled_en.svg/400px-NZL_orthographic_NaturalEarth_labelled_en.svg.png)](https://example.com/wiki/File:NZL_orthographic_NaturalEarth_labelled_en.svg)

Locations of the countries and territories within the [Realm of New Zealand](https://example.com/wiki/Realm_of_New_Zealand "Realm of New Zealand") 

The early European settlers divided New Zealand into [provinces](https://example.com/wiki/Provinces_of_New_Zealand "Provinces of New Zealand"), which had a degree of autonomy.[^132] Because of financial pressures and the desire to consolidate railways, education, land sales and other policies, government was centralised and the provinces were abolished in 1876.[^133] The provinces are remembered in [regional public holidays](https://example.com/wiki/Public_holidays_in_New_Zealand "Public holidays in New Zealand")[^134] and sporting rivalries.[^135] 

Since 1876, various councils have administered local areas under legislation determined by the central government.[^132][^136] In 1989, the government reorganised local government into the current two-tier structure of [regional councils](https://example.com/wiki/Regions_of_New_Zealand "Regions of New Zealand") and [territorial authorities](https://example.com/wiki/Territorial_authorities "Territorial authorities").[^137] The [249 municipalities](https://example.com/wiki/List_of_former_territorial_authorities_in_New_Zealand "List of former territorial authorities in New Zealand")[^137] that existed in 1975 have now been consolidated into 67 territorial authorities and 11 regional councils.[^138] The regional councils' role is to regulate "the natural environment with particular emphasis on [resource management](https://example.com/wiki/Resource_Management_Act_1991 "Resource Management Act 1991")",[^137] while territorial authorities are responsible for sewage, water, local roads, building consents and other local matters.[^139][^140] Five of the territorial councils are [unitary authorities](https://example.com/wiki/Unitary_authority "Unitary authority") and also act as regional councils.[^140] The territorial authorities consist of 13 city councils, 53 [district](https://example.com/wiki/Districts_of_New_Zealand "Districts of New Zealand") councils, and the [Chatham Islands](https://example.com/wiki/Chatham_Islands "Chatham Islands") Council. While officially the Chatham Islands Council is not a unitary authority, it undertakes many functions of a regional council.[^141] 

The Realm of New Zealand, one of 16 [Commonwealth realms](https://example.com/wiki/Commonwealth_realm "Commonwealth realm"),[^142] is the entire area over which the Queen of New Zealand is [sovereign](https://example.com/wiki/Sovereign "Sovereign"), and comprises New Zealand, [Tokelau](https://example.com/wiki/Tokelau "Tokelau"), the [Ross Dependency](https://example.com/wiki/Ross_Dependency "Ross Dependency"), the [Cook Islands](https://example.com/wiki/Cook_Islands "Cook Islands") and [Niue](https://example.com/wiki/Niue "Niue").[^76] The Cook Islands and Niue are self-governing states in [free association](https://example.com/wiki/Associated_state "Associated state") with New Zealand.[^143][^144] The New Zealand Parliament cannot pass legislation for these countries, but with their consent can act on behalf of them in foreign affairs and defence. Tokelau is classified as a [non-self-governing territory](https://example.com/wiki/United_Nations_list_of_Non-Self-Governing_Territories "United Nations list of Non-Self-Governing Territories"), but is administered by a council of three elders (one from each Tokelauan [atoll](https://example.com/wiki/Atoll "Atoll")).[^145] The Ross Dependency is New Zealand's [territorial claim in Antarctica](https://example.com/wiki/Territorial_claims_in_Antarctica "Territorial claims in Antarctica"), where it operates the [Scott Base](https://example.com/wiki/Scott_Base "Scott Base") research facility.[^146] [New Zealand nationality law](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_nationality_law "New Zealand nationality law") treats all parts of the realm equally, so most people born in New Zealand, the Cook Islands, Niue, Tokelau and the Ross Dependency are New Zealand citizens.[^147][^7] 

## Environment

### Geography

[![Islands of New Zealand as seen from satellite](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/New_Zealand_23_October_2002.jpg/170px-New_Zealand_23_October_2002.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:New_Zealand_23_October_2002.jpg)

The snow-capped [Southern Alps](https://example.com/wiki/Southern_Alps "Southern Alps") dominate the South Island, while the North Island's [Northland Peninsula](https://example.com/wiki/Northland_Peninsula "Northland Peninsula") stretches towards the subtropics 

New Zealand is located near the centre of the [water hemisphere](https://example.com/wiki/Water_hemisphere "Water hemisphere") and is made up of two main islands and a number of [smaller islands](https://example.com/wiki/List_of_islands_of_New_Zealand "List of islands of New Zealand"). The two main islands (the [North Island](https://example.com/wiki/North_Island "North Island"), or *Te Ika-a-Māui*, and the [South Island](https://example.com/wiki/South_Island "South Island"), or *Te Waipounamu*) are separated by [Cook Strait](https://example.com/wiki/Cook_Strait "Cook Strait"), 22 kilometres (14 mi) wide at its narrowest point.[^149] Besides the North and South Islands, the five largest inhabited islands are [Stewart Island](https://example.com/wiki/Stewart_Island "Stewart Island") (across the [Foveaux Strait](https://example.com/wiki/Foveaux_Strait "Foveaux Strait")), [Chatham Island](https://example.com/wiki/Chatham_Island "Chatham Island"), [Great Barrier Island](https://example.com/wiki/Great_Barrier_Island "Great Barrier Island") (in the [Hauraki Gulf](https://example.com/wiki/Hauraki_Gulf "Hauraki Gulf")),[^150] [D'Urville Island](https://example.com/wiki/D%27Urville_Island_\(New_Zealand\) "D'Urville Island (New Zealand)") (in the [Marlborough Sounds](https://example.com/wiki/Marlborough_Sounds "Marlborough Sounds"))[^151] and [Waiheke Island](https://example.com/wiki/Waiheke_Island "Waiheke Island") (about 22 km (14 mi) from central Auckland).[^152] 

[![A large mountain with a lake in the foreground](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Mt_Cook%2C_NZ.jpg/220px-Mt_Cook%2C_NZ.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Mt_Cook,_NZ.jpg) 

[Aoraki / Mount Cook](https://example.com/wiki/Aoraki_/_Mount_Cook "Aoraki / Mount Cook") is the highest point of New Zealand, at 3,754 metres 

[![Snow-capped mountain range](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/New_Zealand_moutain_ranges.jpg/220px-New_Zealand_moutain_ranges.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:New_Zealand_moutain_ranges.jpg) 

The Southern Alps stretch for 500 kilometres down the South Island 

New Zealand is long and narrow (over 1,600 kilometres (990 mi) along its north-north-east axis with a maximum width of 400 kilometres (250 mi)),[^153] with about 15,000 km (9,300 mi) of coastline[^154] and a total land area of 268,000 square kilometres (103,500 sq mi).[^155] Because of its far-flung outlying islands and long coastline, the country has extensive marine resources. Its [exclusive economic zone](https://example.com/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone_of_New_Zealand "Exclusive economic zone of New Zealand") is one of the largest in the world, covering more than 15 times its land area.[^156] 

The South Island is the largest landmass of New Zealand. It is divided along its length by the [Southern Alps](https://example.com/wiki/Southern_Alps "Southern Alps").[^157] There are 18 peaks over 3,000 metres (9,800 ft), the highest of which is [Aoraki / Mount Cook](https://example.com/wiki/Aoraki_/_Mount_Cook "Aoraki / Mount Cook") at 3,754 metres (12,316 ft).[^158] [Fiordland](https://example.com/wiki/Fiordland "Fiordland")'s steep mountains and deep [fiords](https://example.com/wiki/Fiord "Fiord") record the extensive ice age glaciation of this southwestern corner of the South Island.[^159] The North Island is less mountainous but is [marked by volcanism](https://example.com/wiki/Volcanology_of_New_Zealand "Volcanology of New Zealand").[^160] The highly active [Taupo Volcanic Zone](https://example.com/wiki/Taupo_Volcanic_Zone "Taupo Volcanic Zone") has formed a large [volcanic plateau](https://example.com/wiki/North_Island_Volcanic_Plateau "North Island Volcanic Plateau"), punctuated by the North Island's highest mountain, [Mount Ruapehu](https://example.com/wiki/Mount_Ruapehu "Mount Ruapehu") (2,797 metres (9,177 ft)). The plateau also hosts the country's largest lake, [Lake Taupo](https://example.com/wiki/Lake_Taupo "Lake Taupo"),[^161] nestled in the [caldera](https://example.com/wiki/Caldera "Caldera") of one of the world's most active [supervolcanoes](https://example.com/wiki/Supervolcano "Supervolcano").[^162] 

The country owes its varied topography, and perhaps even its emergence above the waves, to the dynamic boundary it straddles between the [Pacific](https://example.com/wiki/Pacific_Plate "Pacific Plate") and [Indo-Australian Plates](https://example.com/wiki/Indo-Australian_Plate "Indo-Australian Plate").[^163] New Zealand is part of [Zealandia](https://example.com/wiki/Zealandia "Zealandia"), a [microcontinent](https://example.com/wiki/Microcontinent "Microcontinent") nearly half the size of Australia that gradually submerged after breaking away from the [Gondwanan](https://example.com/wiki/Gondwana "Gondwana") supercontinent.[^164] About 25 million years ago, a shift in [plate tectonic](https://example.com/wiki/Plate_tectonic "Plate tectonic") movements began to [contort and crumple](https://example.com/wiki/Kaikoura_Orogeny "Kaikoura Orogeny") the region. This is now most evident in the Southern Alps, formed by [compression of the crust](https://example.com/wiki/Continental_collision "Continental collision") beside the [Alpine Fault](https://example.com/wiki/Alpine_Fault "Alpine Fault"). Elsewhere the plate boundary involves the [subduction](https://example.com/wiki/Subduction "Subduction") of one plate under the other, producing the [Puysegur Trench](https://example.com/wiki/Puysegur_Trench "Puysegur Trench") to the south, the [Hikurangi Trench](https://example.com/wiki/Hikurangi_Trench "Hikurangi Trench") east of the North Island, and the [Kermadec](https://example.com/wiki/Kermadec_Trench "Kermadec Trench") and [Tonga Trenches](https://example.com/wiki/Tonga_Trench "Tonga Trench")[^165] further north.[^163] 

New Zealand is part of a region known as [Australasia](https://example.com/wiki/Australasia "Australasia"), together with Australia.[^166] It also forms the southwestern extremity of the geographic and ethnographic region called [Polynesia](https://example.com/wiki/Polynesia "Polynesia").[^167] The term [Oceania](https://example.com/wiki/Oceania "Oceania") is often used to denote the wider region encompassing the [Australian continent](https://example.com/wiki/Australia_\(continent\) "Australia (continent)"), New Zealand and various islands in the Pacific Ocean that are not included in the [seven-continent](https://example.com/wiki/Continent "Continent") model.[^168]

### Climate

New Zealand's climate is predominantly temperate [maritime](https://example.com/wiki/Oceanic_climate "Oceanic climate") ([Köppen](https://example.com/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification "Köppen climate classification"): Cfb), with mean annual temperatures ranging from 10 °C (50 °F) in the south to 16 °C (61 °F) in the north.[^169] Historical [maxima and minima](https://example.com/wiki/Maxima_and_minima "Maxima and minima") are 42.4 °C (108.32 °F) in [Rangiora](https://example.com/wiki/Rangiora "Rangiora"), [Canterbury](https://example.com/wiki/Canterbury,_New_Zealand "Canterbury, New Zealand") and −25.6 °C (−14.08 °F) in [Ranfurly](https://example.com/wiki/Ranfurly,_New_Zealand "Ranfurly, New Zealand"), [Otago](https://example.com/wiki/Otago "Otago").[^170] Conditions vary sharply across regions from extremely wet on the [West Coast](https://example.com/wiki/West_Coast,_New_Zealand "West Coast, New Zealand") of the South Island to almost [semi-arid](https://example.com/wiki/Semi-arid_climate "Semi-arid climate") in [Central Otago](https://example.com/wiki/Central_Otago "Central Otago") and the [Mackenzie Basin](https://example.com/wiki/Mackenzie_Basin "Mackenzie Basin") of inland Canterbury and [subtropical](https://example.com/wiki/Subtropical "Subtropical") in [Northland](https://example.com/wiki/Northland_Peninsula "Northland Peninsula").[^171] Of the seven largest cities, [Christchurch](https://example.com/wiki/Christchurch "Christchurch") is the driest, receiving on average only 640 millimetres (25 in) of rain per year and Wellington the wettest, receiving almost twice that amount.[^172] Auckland, Wellington and Christchurch all receive a yearly average of more than 2,000 hours of sunshine. The southern and southwestern parts of the South Island have a cooler and cloudier climate, with around 1,400–1,600 hours; the northern and northeastern parts of the South Island are the sunniest areas of the country and receive about 2,400–2,500 hours.[^173] The general snow season is early June until early October, though [cold snaps](https://example.com/wiki/Cold_snap "Cold snap") can occur outside this season.[^174] Snowfall is common in the eastern and southern parts of the South Island and mountain areas across the country.[^169]

### Biodiversity

[![Kiwi amongst sticks](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/TeTuatahianui.jpg/170px-TeTuatahianui.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:TeTuatahianui.jpg)

The endemic flightless [kiwi](https://example.com/wiki/Kiwi "Kiwi") is a national icon. 

New Zealand's [geographic isolation](https://example.com/wiki/Geographic_isolation "Geographic isolation") for 80 million years[^176] and island [biogeography](https://example.com/wiki/Biogeography "Biogeography") has influenced evolution of the country's species of [animals](https://example.com/wiki/Fauna "Fauna"), [fungi](https://example.com/wiki/Fungus "Fungus") and [plants](https://example.com/wiki/Flora "Flora"). Physical isolation has caused biological isolation, resulting in a dynamic evolutionary ecology with examples of very distinctive plants and animals as well as populations of widespread species.[^177][^178] About 82% of New Zealand's indigenous [vascular plants](https://example.com/wiki/Vascular_plant "Vascular plant") are [endemic](https://example.com/wiki/Endemism "Endemism"), covering 1,944 species across 65 [genera](https://example.com/wiki/Genus "Genus").[^179][^180] The number of fungi recorded from New Zealand, including lichen-forming species, is not known, nor is the proportion of those fungi which are endemic, but one estimate suggests there are about 2,300 species of lichen-forming fungi in New Zealand[^179] and 40% of these are endemic.[^181] The two main types of forest are those dominated by broadleaf trees with emergent [podocarps](https://example.com/wiki/Podocarp "Podocarp"), or by [southern beech](https://example.com/wiki/Nothofagus "Nothofagus") in cooler climates.[^182] The remaining vegetation types consist of grasslands, the majority of which are [tussock](https://example.com/wiki/Tussock_grasslands_of_New_Zealand "Tussock grasslands of New Zealand").[^183] 

Before the arrival of humans, an estimated 80% of the land was covered in forest, with only [high alpine](https://example.com/wiki/Tree_line "Tree line"), wet, infertile and volcanic areas without trees.[^184] Massive [deforestation](https://example.com/wiki/Deforestation_in_New_Zealand "Deforestation in New Zealand") occurred after humans arrived, with around half the forest cover lost to fire after Polynesian settlement.[^185] Much of the remaining forest fell after European settlement, being logged or cleared to make room for pastoral farming, leaving forest occupying only 23% of the land.[^186] 

[![An artist's rendition of a Haast's eagle attacking two moa](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Giant_Haasts_eagle_attacking_New_Zealand_moa.jpg/220px-Giant_Haasts_eagle_attacking_New_Zealand_moa.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Giant_Haasts_eagle_attacking_New_Zealand_moa.jpg)

The giant [Haast's eagle](https://example.com/wiki/Haast%27s_eagle "Haast's eagle") died out when humans hunted its main prey, the [moa](https://example.com/wiki/Moa "Moa"), to extinction. 

The forests were dominated by [birds](https://example.com/wiki/Birds_of_New_Zealand "Birds of New Zealand"), and the lack of mammalian predators led to some like the [kiwi](https://example.com/wiki/Kiwi "Kiwi"), [kakapo](https://example.com/wiki/Kakapo "Kakapo"), [weka](https://example.com/wiki/Weka "Weka") and [takahē](https://example.com/wiki/Takah%C4%93 "Takahē") evolving [flightlessness](https://example.com/wiki/Flightless_bird "Flightless bird").[^187] The arrival of humans, associated changes to habitat, and the introduction of [rats](https://example.com/wiki/Polynesian_rat "Polynesian rat"), ferrets and other mammals led to the [extinction](https://example.com/wiki/List_of_New_Zealand_animals_extinct_in_the_Holocene "List of New Zealand animals extinct in the Holocene") of many bird species, including [large birds](https://example.com/wiki/Megafauna "Megafauna") like the [moa](https://example.com/wiki/Moa "Moa") and [Haast's eagle](https://example.com/wiki/Haast%27s_eagle "Haast's eagle").[^188][^189] 

Other indigenous animals are represented by reptiles ([tuatara](https://example.com/wiki/Tuatara "Tuatara"), [skinks](https://example.com/wiki/Skink "Skink") and [geckos](https://example.com/wiki/List_of_geckos_of_New_Zealand "List of geckos of New Zealand")), [frogs](https://example.com/wiki/Leiopelmatidae "Leiopelmatidae"),[^190] [spiders](https://example.com/wiki/Spiders_of_New_Zealand "Spiders of New Zealand"),[^191] insects ([weta](https://example.com/wiki/Weta "Weta"))[^192] and snails.[^193] Some, such as the tuatara, are so unique that they have been called [living fossils](https://example.com/wiki/Living_fossil "Living fossil").[^194] Three species of bats ([one](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_greater_short-tailed_bat "New Zealand greater short-tailed bat") since extinct) were the only sign of native land mammals in New Zealand until the 2006 discovery of bones from [a unique, mouse-sized land mammal](https://example.com/wiki/Saint_Bathans_mammal "Saint Bathans mammal") at least 16 million years old.[^195][^196] Marine mammals however are abundant, with almost half the world's [cetaceans](https://example.com/wiki/Cetacea "Cetacea") (whales, dolphins, and [porpoises](https://example.com/wiki/Porpoise "Porpoise")) and large numbers of [fur seals](https://example.com/wiki/Fur_seal "Fur seal") reported in New Zealand waters.[^197] Many seabirds breed in New Zealand, a third of them unique to the country.[^198] More [penguin](https://example.com/wiki/List_of_birds_of_New_Zealand#Penguins "List of birds of New Zealand") species are found in New Zealand than in any other country.[^199] 

Since human arrival, almost half of the country's vertebrate species have become extinct, including at least fifty-one birds, three frogs, three lizards, one freshwater fish, and one bat. Others are endangered or have had their range severely reduced.[^188] However, New Zealand conservationists have pioneered several methods to help threatened wildlife recover, including island sanctuaries, pest control, wildlife translocation, fostering, and ecological [restoration of islands](https://example.com/wiki/Island_restoration "Island restoration") and [other selected areas](https://example.com/wiki/Ecological_island "Ecological island").[^200][^201][^202][^203] 

## Economy

[![Boats docked in blue-green water. Plate glass skyscrapers rising up in the background.](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Auckland_Waterfrt.jpg/220px-Auckland_Waterfrt.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Auckland_Waterfrt.jpg)

[Waterfront](https://example.com/wiki/Auckland_waterfront "Auckland waterfront") along [Auckland CBD](https://example.com/wiki/Auckland_CBD "Auckland CBD"), a major hub of economic activity 

New Zealand has an [advanced](https://example.com/wiki/Advanced_economy "Advanced economy") [market economy](https://example.com/wiki/Market_economy "Market economy"),[^204] ranked 16th in the 2018 [Human Development Index](https://example.com/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index")[^19] and third in the 2018 [Index of Economic Freedom](https://example.com/wiki/Index_of_Economic_Freedom "Index of Economic Freedom").[^205] It is a [high-income economy](https://example.com/wiki/High-income_economy "High-income economy") with a [nominal](https://example.com/wiki/Nominal_value "Nominal value") [gross domestic product](https://example.com/wiki/Gross_domestic_product "Gross domestic product") (GDP) per capita of [US$](https://example.com/wiki/United_States_dollar "United States dollar")36,254.[^17] The currency is the [New Zealand dollar](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_dollar "New Zealand dollar"), informally known as the "Kiwi dollar"; it also circulates in the Cook Islands (see [Cook Islands dollar](https://example.com/wiki/Cook_Islands_dollar "Cook Islands dollar")), Niue, Tokelau, and the [Pitcairn Islands](https://example.com/wiki/Pitcairn_Islands "Pitcairn Islands").[^206] 

Historically, extractive industries have contributed strongly to New Zealand's economy, focussing at different times on sealing, whaling, [flax](https://example.com/wiki/Phormium "Phormium"), gold, [kauri gum](https://example.com/wiki/Kauri_gum "Kauri gum"), and native timber.[^207] The first shipment of refrigerated meat on the *[Dunedin](https://example.com/wiki/Dunedin_\(ship\) "Dunedin (ship)")* in 1882 led to the establishment of meat and dairy exports to Britain, a trade which provided the basis for strong economic growth in New Zealand.[^208] High demand for agricultural products from the United Kingdom and the United States helped New Zealanders achieve higher living standards than both Australia and Western Europe in the 1950s and 1960s.[^209] In 1973, New Zealand's export market was reduced when the United Kingdom joined the [European Economic Community](https://example.com/wiki/European_Economic_Community "European Economic Community")[^210] and other compounding factors, such as the [1973 oil](https://example.com/wiki/1973_oil_crisis "1973 oil crisis") and [1979 energy](https://example.com/wiki/1979_oil_crisis "1979 oil crisis") crises, led to a severe [economic depression](https://example.com/wiki/Depression_\(economics\) "Depression (economics)").[^211] Living standards in New Zealand fell behind those of Australia and Western Europe, and by 1982 New Zealand had the lowest per-capita income of all the developed nations surveyed by [the World Bank](https://example.com/wiki/World_Bank_Group "World Bank Group").[^212] In the mid-1980s New Zealand deregulated its [agricultural sector](https://example.com/wiki/Agriculture_in_New_Zealand "Agriculture in New Zealand") by phasing out [subsidies](https://example.com/wiki/Agricultural_subsidy "Agricultural subsidy") over a three-year period.[^213][^214] Since 1984, successive governments engaged in major [macroeconomic](https://example.com/wiki/Macroeconomic "Macroeconomic") restructuring (known first as [Rogernomics](https://example.com/wiki/Rogernomics "Rogernomics") and then [Ruthanasia](https://example.com/wiki/Ruthanasia "Ruthanasia")), rapidly transforming New Zealand from a [protected](https://example.com/wiki/Protectionism "Protectionism") and highly regulated economy to a liberalised [free-trade](https://example.com/wiki/Free-trade "Free-trade") economy.[^215][^216] 

[![Blue water against a backdrop of snow-capped mountains](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/MilfordSound.jpg/220px-MilfordSound.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:MilfordSound.jpg)

[Milford Sound](https://example.com/wiki/Milford_Sound "Milford Sound") is one of New Zealand's most famous tourist destinations.[^217] 

Unemployment peaked above 10% in 1991 and 1992,[^218] following the [1987 share market crash](https://example.com/wiki/Black_Monday_\(1987\) "Black Monday (1987)"), but eventually fell to a record low (since 1986) of 3.7% in 2007 (ranking third from twenty-seven comparable OECD nations).[^218] However, the [global financial crisis](https://example.com/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932008 "Financial crisis of 2007–2008") that followed had a major impact on New Zealand, with the GDP shrinking for five consecutive quarters, the longest recession in over thirty years,[^219][^220] and unemployment rising back to 7% in late 2009.[^221] Unemployment rates for different age groups follow similar trends, but are consistently higher among youth. In the December 2014 quarter, the general unemployment rate was around 5.8%, while the unemployment rate for youth aged 15 to 21 was 15.6%.[^218] New Zealand has experienced a series of "[brain drains](https://example.com/wiki/Brain_drain "Brain drain")" since the 1970s[^222] that still continue today.[^223] Nearly one quarter of highly skilled workers live overseas, mostly in Australia and Britain, which is the largest proportion from any developed nation.[^224] In recent decades, however, a "brain gain" has brought in educated professionals from Europe and less developed countries.[^225][^226] Today New Zealand's economy benefits from a high level of [innovation](https://example.com/wiki/Innovation "Innovation").[^227] 

### Trade

New Zealand is heavily dependent on international trade,[^228] particularly in agricultural products.[^229] Exports account for 24% of its output,[^154] making New Zealand vulnerable to international commodity prices and global [economic slowdowns](https://example.com/wiki/Recession "Recession"). Food products made up 55% of the value of all the country's exports in 2014; wood was the second largest earner (7%).[^230] New Zealand's main trading partners, as at June 2018, are China ([NZ$](https://example.com/wiki/NZ$ "NZ$")27.8b), Australia ($26.2b), the [European Union](https://example.com/wiki/European_Union "European Union") ($22.9b), the United States ($17.6b), and Japan ($8.4b).[^231] On 7 April 2008, New Zealand and China signed the [New Zealand–China Free Trade Agreement](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand%E2%80%93China_Free_Trade_Agreement "New Zealand–China Free Trade Agreement"), the first such agreement China has signed with a developed country.[^232] The service sector is the largest sector in the economy, followed by manufacturing and construction and then farming and raw material extraction.[^154] [Tourism](https://example.com/wiki/Tourism_in_New_Zealand "Tourism in New Zealand") plays a significant role in the economy, contributing $12.9 billion (or 5.6%) to New Zealand's total GDP and supporting 7.5% of the total workforce in 2016.[^233] International visitor arrivals are expected to increase at a rate of 5.4% annually up to 2022.[^233] 

[![A Romney ewe with her two lambs](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Fauna_de_Nueva_Zelanda07.JPG/220px-Fauna_de_Nueva_Zelanda07.JPG)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Fauna_de_Nueva_Zelanda07.JPG)

Wool has historically been one of New Zealand's major exports. 

Wool was New Zealand's major agricultural export during the late 19th century.[^207] Even as late as the 1960s it made up over a third of all export revenues,[^207] but since then its price has steadily dropped relative to other commodities[^234] and wool is no longer profitable for many farmers.[^235] In contrast [dairy farming](https://example.com/wiki/Dairy_farming_in_New_Zealand "Dairy farming in New Zealand") increased, with the number of dairy cows doubling between 1990 and 2007,[^236] to become New Zealand's largest export earner.[^237] In the year to June 2018, dairy products accounted for 17.7% ($14.1 billion) of total exports,[^231] and the country's largest company, [Fonterra](https://example.com/wiki/Fonterra "Fonterra"), controls almost one-third of the international dairy trade.[^238] Other exports in 2017-18 were meat (8.8%), wood and wood products (6.2%), fruit (3.6%), machinery (2.2%) and wine (2.1%).[^231] [New Zealand's wine](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_wine "New Zealand wine") industry has followed a similar trend to dairy, the number of vineyards doubling over the same period,[^239] overtaking wool exports for the first time in 2007.[^240][^241] 

### Infrastructure

[![A mid-size jet airliner in flight. The plane livery is all-black and features a New Zealand silver fern mark.](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Air_New_Zealand%2C_Boeing_787-9_ZK-NZE_%27All_Blacks%27_NRT_%2827091961041%29.jpg/220px-Air_New_Zealand%2C_Boeing_787-9_ZK-NZE_%27All_Blacks%27_NRT_%2827091961041%29.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Air_New_Zealand,_Boeing_787-9_ZK-NZE_%27All_Blacks%27_NRT_\(27091961041\).jpg)

A [Boeing 787-9 Dreamliner](https://example.com/wiki/Boeing_787_Dreamliner "Boeing 787 Dreamliner") of [Air New Zealand](https://example.com/wiki/Air_New_Zealand "Air New Zealand"), the flag carrier of New Zealand 

In 2015, [renewable energy](https://example.com/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_New_Zealand "Renewable energy in New Zealand"), primarily [geothermal](https://example.com/wiki/Geothermal_power_in_New_Zealand "Geothermal power in New Zealand") and [hydroelectric power](https://example.com/wiki/Hydroelectric_power_in_New_Zealand "Hydroelectric power in New Zealand"), generated 40.1% of [New Zealand's gross energy](https://example.com/wiki/Energy_in_New_Zealand "Energy in New Zealand") supply.[^242] Geothermal power alone accounted for 22% of New Zealand's energy in 2015.[^242] 

The provision of [water supply and sanitation](https://example.com/wiki/Water_supply_and_sanitation_in_New_Zealand "Water supply and sanitation in New Zealand") is generally of good quality. Regional authorities provide water abstraction, treatment and distribution infrastructure to most developed areas.[^243][^244] 

[New Zealand's transport](https://example.com/wiki/Transport_in_New_Zealand "Transport in New Zealand") network comprises 94,000 kilometres (58,410 mi) of roads, including 199 kilometres (124 mi) of motorways,[^245] and 4,128 kilometres (2,565 mi) of railway lines.[^154] Most major cities and towns are linked by bus services, although the private car is the predominant mode of transport.[^246] The [railways](https://example.com/wiki/Rail_transport_in_New_Zealand "Rail transport in New Zealand") were privatised in 1993, but were re-nationalised by the government in stages between 2004 and 2008. The state-owned enterprise [KiwiRail](https://example.com/wiki/KiwiRail "KiwiRail") now operates the railways, with the exception of commuter services in Auckland and Wellington which are operated by [Transdev](https://example.com/wiki/Transdev_Auckland "Transdev Auckland")[^247] and [Metlink](https://example.com/wiki/Public_transport_in_the_Wellington_Region "Public transport in the Wellington Region"),[^248] respectively. Railways run the length of the country, although most lines now carry freight rather than passengers.[^249] Most international visitors arrive via air[^250] and New Zealand has [six international airports](https://example.com/wiki/List_of_airports_in_New_Zealand "List of airports in New Zealand"), but currently only the [Auckland](https://example.com/wiki/Auckland_Airport "Auckland Airport") and [Christchurch airports](https://example.com/wiki/Christchurch_International_Airport "Christchurch International Airport") connect directly with countries other than Australia or Fiji.[^251] 

The [New Zealand Post Office](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Post_Office "New Zealand Post Office") had a monopoly over [telecommunications in New Zealand](https://example.com/wiki/Telecommunications_in_New_Zealand "Telecommunications in New Zealand") until 1987 when [Telecom New Zealand](https://example.com/wiki/Spark_New_Zealand "Spark New Zealand") was formed, initially as a state-owned enterprise and then privatised in 1990.[^252] [Chorus](https://example.com/wiki/Chorus_Limited "Chorus Limited"), which was split from Telecom (now Spark) in 2011,[^253] still owns the majority of the telecommunications infrastructure, but competition from other providers has increased.[^252] A large-scale rollout of gigabit-capable [fibre to the premises](https://example.com/wiki/Fibre_to_the_premises "Fibre to the premises"), branded as [Ultra-Fast Broadband](https://example.com/wiki/Ultra-Fast_Broadband "Ultra-Fast Broadband"), began in 2009 with a target of being available to 87% of the population by 2022.[^254] As of 2017, the United Nations [International Telecommunication Union](https://example.com/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union "International Telecommunication Union") ranks New Zealand 13th in the development of information and communications infrastructure.[^255] 

## Demography

The [2013 New Zealand census](https://example.com/wiki/2013_New_Zealand_census "2013 New Zealand census") enumerated a resident population of 4,242,048, an increase of 5.3% over the 2006 figure.[^256][^8] As of September 2019, the total population has risen to an estimated 4,933,210.[^16] 

New Zealand is a predominantly urban country, with 73.0% of the population living in the seventeen main [urban areas](https://example.com/wiki/Urban_area "Urban area") (i.e. population 30,000 or greater) and 55.1% living in the four largest cities of [Auckland](https://example.com/wiki/Auckland "Auckland"), [Christchurch](https://example.com/wiki/Christchurch "Christchurch"), [Wellington](https://example.com/wiki/Wellington "Wellington"), and [Hamilton](https://example.com/wiki/Hamilton,_New_Zealand "Hamilton, New Zealand").[^258] New Zealand cities generally rank highly on international livability measures. For instance, in 2016 Auckland was ranked the world's third [most liveable city](https://example.com/wiki/Most_liveable_city "Most liveable city") and Wellington the twelfth by the [Mercer Quality of Living Survey](https://example.com/wiki/Mercer_Quality_of_Living_Survey "Mercer Quality of Living Survey").[^259] 

Life expectancy for New Zealanders in 2012 was 84 years for females, and 80.2 years for males.[^260] Life expectancy at birth is forecast to increase from 80 years to 85 years in 2050 and infant mortality is expected to decline.[^261] New Zealand's [fertility rate](https://example.com/wiki/Fertility_rate "Fertility rate") of 2.1 is relatively high for a developed country, and natural births account for a significant proportion of [population growth](https://example.com/wiki/Replacement_fertility_rate "Replacement fertility rate"). Consequently, the country has a young population compared to most industrialised nations, with 20% of New Zealanders being 14 years old or younger.[^154] In 2018 the [median age](https://example.com/wiki/Median_age "Median age") of the New Zealand population was 38.1 years.[^262] By 2050 the median age is projected to rise to 43 years and the percentage of people 60 years of age and older to rise from 18% to 29%.[^261] In 2008 the leading cause of premature death was [cancer](https://example.com/wiki/Cancer "Cancer"), at 29.8%, followed by [ischaemic heart disease](https://example.com/wiki/Ischaemic_heart_disease "Ischaemic heart disease"), 19.7%, and then [cerebrovascular disease](https://example.com/wiki/Cerebrovascular_disease "Cerebrovascular disease"), 9.2%.[^263] As of 2016, total expenditure on [health care](https://example.com/wiki/Health_care_in_New_Zealand "Health care in New Zealand") (including private sector spending) is 9.2% of GDP.[^264]  
 

### Ethnicity and immigration

[![Pedestrians crossing a wide street which is flanked by storefronts](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Queen_Street_Midtown_Auckland.jpg/220px-Queen_Street_Midtown_Auckland.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Queen_Street_Midtown_Auckland.jpg)

Pedestrians on [Queen Street](https://example.com/wiki/Queen_Street,_Auckland "Queen Street, Auckland") in Auckland, an ethnically diverse city 

In the 2013 census, 74.0% of New Zealand residents identified ethnically as European, and 14.9% as Māori. Other major ethnic groups include [Asian](https://example.com/wiki/Asian_New_Zealanders "Asian New Zealanders") (11.8%) and Pacific peoples (7.4%), two-thirds of whom live in the [Auckland Region](https://example.com/wiki/Auckland_Region "Auckland Region").[^266][^3] The population has become more diverse in recent decades: in 1961, the census reported that the population of New Zealand was 92% European and 7% Māori, with Asian and Pacific minorities sharing the remaining 1%.[^267] 

While the [demonym](https://example.com/wiki/Demonym "Demonym") for a New Zealand citizen is New Zealander, the informal "[Kiwi](https://example.com/wiki/Kiwi_\(people\) "Kiwi (people)")" is commonly used both internationally[^268] and by locals.[^269] The Māori loanword [Pākehā](https://example.com/wiki/P%C4%81keh%C4%81 "Pākehā") has been used to refer to [New Zealanders of European descent](https://example.com/wiki/European_New_Zealanders "European New Zealanders"), although others reject this appellation.[^270][^271] The word Pākehā today is increasingly used to refer to all non-Polynesian New Zealanders.[^272] 

The Māori were the first people to reach New Zealand, followed by the early [European settlers](https://example.com/wiki/P%C4%81keh%C4%81_settlers "Pākehā settlers"). Following colonisation, immigrants were predominantly from Britain, Ireland and Australia because of restrictive policies similar to the [White Australia policy](https://example.com/wiki/White_Australia_policy "White Australia policy").[^273] There was also significant Dutch, [Dalmatian](https://example.com/wiki/Dalmatia "Dalmatia"),[^274] German, and Italian immigration, together with indirect European immigration through Australia, North America, South America and South Africa.[^275][^276] Net migration increased after the Second World War; in the 1970s and 1980s policies were relaxed and immigration from Asia was promoted.[^276][^277] In 2009–10, an annual target of 45,000–50,000 permanent residence approvals was set by the New Zealand Immigration Service—more than one new migrant for every 100 New Zealand residents.[^278] Just over 25% of New Zealand's population was born overseas, with the majority (52%) living in the Auckland Region. The United Kingdom remains the largest source of New Zealand's overseas population, with a quarter of all overseas-born New Zealanders born there; other major sources of New Zealand's overseas-born population are China, India, Australia, South Africa, Fiji and Samoa.[^279] The number of fee-paying [international students](https://example.com/wiki/International_student "International student") increased sharply in the late 1990s, with more than 20,000 studying in public [tertiary institutions](https://example.com/wiki/Tertiary_education "Tertiary education") in 2002.[^280]

English is the predominant language in New Zealand, spoken by 96.1% of the population.[^282] [New Zealand English](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_English "New Zealand English") is similar to [Australian English](https://example.com/wiki/Australian_English "Australian English") and many speakers from the Northern Hemisphere are unable to tell the [accents](https://example.com/wiki/Accent_\(sociolinguistics\) "Accent (sociolinguistics)") apart.[^283] The most prominent differences between the New Zealand English dialect and other English dialects are the shifts in the short front vowels: the short-"i" sound (as in "kit") has centralised towards the [schwa](https://example.com/wiki/Schwa "Schwa") sound (the "a" in "comma" and "about"); the short-"e" sound (as in "dress") has moved towards the short-"i" sound; and the short-"a" sound (as in "trap") has moved to the short-"e" sound.[^284] 

After the Second World War, Māori were discouraged from speaking their own language (*[te reo Māori](https://example.com/wiki/Te_reo_M%C4%81ori "Te reo Māori")*) in schools and workplaces and it existed as a community language only in a few remote areas.[^285] It has recently undergone a process of revitalisation,[^286] being declared one of New Zealand's official languages in 1987,[^287] and is spoken by 3.7% of the population.[^282][^9] There are now Māori language immersion schools and two television channels that broadcast predominantly in Māori.[^289] [Many places](https://example.com/wiki/List_of_dual_place_names_in_New_Zealand "List of dual place names in New Zealand") have both their Māori and English names officially recognised.[^290] 

As recorded in the 2013 census,[^282] [Samoan](https://example.com/wiki/Samoan_language "Samoan language") is the most widely spoken non-official language (2.2%),[^10] followed by [Hindi](https://example.com/wiki/Hindi "Hindi") (1.7%), "Northern Chinese" (including [Mandarin](https://example.com/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese "Mandarin Chinese"), 1.3%) and French (1.2%). 20,235 people (0.5%) reported the ability to use [New Zealand Sign Language](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Sign_Language "New Zealand Sign Language"). It was declared one of New Zealand's official languages in 2006.[^291] 

### Religion

[![Simple white building with two red domed towers](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Ratana_Church_Raetihi.jpg/170px-Ratana_Church_Raetihi.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Ratana_Church_Raetihi.jpg)

A [Rātana](https://example.com/wiki/R%C4%81tana "Rātana") church on a hill near [Raetihi](https://example.com/wiki/Raetihi "Raetihi"). The two-tower construction is characteristic of Rātana buildings. 

[Christianity](https://example.com/wiki/Christianity_in_New_Zealand "Christianity in New Zealand") is the predominant religion in New Zealand, although its society is among the most [secular](https://example.com/wiki/Secular "Secular") in the world.[^292][^293] In the 2018 census, 51.4% of the population identified with one or more religions, including 38.6% identifying as Christians. Another 48.6% indicated that they had no religion.[^11][^294] The main Christian denominations are, by number of adherents, [Roman Catholicism](https://example.com/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_New_Zealand "Catholic Church in New Zealand") (10.1%), [Anglicanism](https://example.com/wiki/Anglican_Church_in_Aotearoa,_New_Zealand_and_Polynesia "Anglican Church in Aotearoa, New Zealand and Polynesia") (6.8%), [Presbyterianism](https://example.com/wiki/Presbyterian_Church_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand "Presbyterian Church of Aotearoa New Zealand") (5.5%) and "Christian not further defined" (i.e. people identifying as Christian but not stating the denomination, 6.6%).[^294] The Māori-based [Ringatū](https://example.com/wiki/Ringat%C5%AB "Ringatū") and [Rātana](https://example.com/wiki/R%C4%81tana "Rātana") religions (1.3%) are also Christian in origin.[^295][^296] Immigration and demographic change in recent decades has contributed to the growth of minority religions,[^297] such as [Hinduism](https://example.com/wiki/Hinduism_in_New_Zealand "Hinduism in New Zealand") (2.6%), [Buddhism](https://example.com/wiki/Buddhism_in_New_Zealand "Buddhism in New Zealand") (1.1%), [Islam](https://example.com/wiki/Islam_in_New_Zealand "Islam in New Zealand") (1.3%) and [Sikhism](https://example.com/wiki/Sikhism_in_New_Zealand "Sikhism in New Zealand") (0.5%).[^295] The Auckland Region exhibited the greatest religious diversity.[^295] 

### Education

Primary and secondary schooling is compulsory for children aged 6 to 16, with the majority attending from the age of 5.[^298] There are 13 school years and attending [state (public) schools](https://example.com/wiki/State_school "State school") is free to New Zealand citizens and permanent residents from a person's 5th birthday to the end of the calendar year following their 19th birthday.[^299] New Zealand has an adult literacy rate of 99%,[^154] and over half of the population aged 15 to 29 hold a tertiary qualification.[^298] There are five types of government-owned tertiary institutions: universities, colleges of education, [polytechnics](https://example.com/wiki/Institute_of_technology#New_Zealand "Institute of technology"), specialist colleges, and [wānanga](https://example.com/wiki/W%C4%81nanga "Wānanga"),[^300] in addition to private training establishments.[^301] In the adult population 14.2% have a [bachelor's degree](https://example.com/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree "Bachelor's degree") or higher, 30.4% have some form of secondary qualification as their highest qualification and 22.4% have no formal qualification.[^302] The OECD's [Programme for International Student Assessment](https://example.com/wiki/Programme_for_International_Student_Assessment "Programme for International Student Assessment") ranks New Zealand's education system as the seventh best in the world, with students performing exceptionally well in reading, mathematics and science.[^303] 

## Culture

[![Tall wooden carving showing Kupe above two tentacled sea creatures](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/KupeWheke.jpg/150px-KupeWheke.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:KupeWheke.jpg "Use the scrollbar to see the full image.") 

Late 20th-century house-post depicting the navigator [Kupe](https://example.com/wiki/Kupe "Kupe") fighting two sea creatures 

Early Māori adapted the tropically based east [Polynesian culture](https://example.com/wiki/Polynesian_culture "Polynesian culture") in line with the challenges associated with a larger and more diverse environment, eventually developing their own distinctive culture. Social organisation was largely communal with families (*[whānau](https://example.com/wiki/Wh%C4%81nau "Whānau")*), subtribes (*hapū*) and tribes (*iwi*) ruled by a chief (*[rangatira](https://example.com/wiki/Rangatira "Rangatira")*), whose position was subject to the community's approval.[^304] The British and Irish immigrants brought aspects of their own culture to New Zealand and also influenced Māori culture,[^305][^306] particularly with the introduction of Christianity.[^307] However, Māori still regard their allegiance to tribal groups as a vital part of [their identity](https://example.com/wiki/Cultural_identity "Cultural identity"), and Māori kinship roles resemble [those of other Polynesian peoples](https://example.com/wiki/Hawaiian_kinship "Hawaiian kinship").[^308] More recently [American](https://example.com/wiki/Culture_of_the_United_States "Culture of the United States"), [Australian](https://example.com/wiki/Culture_of_Australia "Culture of Australia"), [Asian](https://example.com/wiki/Culture_of_Asia "Culture of Asia") and other [European cultures](https://example.com/wiki/Culture_of_Europe "Culture of Europe") have exerted influence on New Zealand. Non-Māori Polynesian cultures are also apparent, with [Pasifika](https://example.com/wiki/Pasifika_Festival "Pasifika Festival"), the world's largest Polynesian festival, now an annual event in Auckland.[^309] 

The largely rural life in early New Zealand led to the image of New Zealanders being rugged, industrious problem solvers.[^310] Modesty was expected and enforced through the "[tall poppy syndrome](https://example.com/wiki/Tall_poppy_syndrome "Tall poppy syndrome")", where high achievers received harsh criticism.[^311] At the time New Zealand was not known as an intellectual country.[^312] From the early 20th century until the late 1960s, Māori culture was suppressed by the attempted [assimilation](https://example.com/wiki/Forced_assimilation "Forced assimilation") of Māori into British New Zealanders.[^285] In the 1960s, as tertiary education became more available and [cities expanded](https://example.com/wiki/Urbanization "Urbanization")[^313] urban culture began to dominate.[^314] However, rural imagery and themes are common in New Zealand's art, literature and media.[^315] 

[New Zealand's national symbols](https://example.com/wiki/National_symbols_of_New_Zealand "National symbols of New Zealand") are influenced by natural, historical, and Māori sources. The [silver fern](https://example.com/wiki/Silver_fern "Silver fern") is an emblem appearing on army insignia and sporting team uniforms.[^316] Certain items of popular culture thought to be unique to New Zealand are called "[Kiwiana](https://example.com/wiki/Kiwiana "Kiwiana")".[^316] 

### Art

As part of the resurgence of Māori culture, the traditional crafts of carving and weaving are now more widely practised and Māori artists are increasing in number and influence.[^317] Most Māori carvings feature human figures, generally with three fingers and either a natural-looking, detailed head or a grotesque head.[^318] Surface patterns consisting of spirals, ridges, notches and fish scales decorate most carvings.[^319] The pre-eminent Māori architecture consisted of carved meeting houses (*[wharenui](https://example.com/wiki/Wharenui "Wharenui")*) decorated with symbolic carvings and illustrations. These buildings were originally designed to be constantly rebuilt, changing and adapting to different whims or needs.[^320] 

Māori decorated the white wood of buildings, canoes and cenotaphs using red (a mixture of red [ochre](https://example.com/wiki/Ochre "Ochre") and shark fat) and black (made from soot) paint and painted pictures of birds, reptiles and other designs on cave walls.[^321] Māori tattoos (*[moko](https://example.com/wiki/T%C4%81_moko "Tā moko")*) consisting of coloured soot mixed with gum were cut into the flesh with a bone chisel.[^322] Since European arrival paintings and photographs have been dominated by landscapes, originally not as works of art but as factual portrayals of New Zealand.[^323] Portraits of Māori were also common, with early painters often portraying them as "[noble savages](https://example.com/wiki/Noble_savage "Noble savage")", exotic beauties or friendly natives.[^323] The country's isolation delayed the influence of European artistic trends allowing local artists to develop their own distinctive style of [regionalism](https://example.com/wiki/Regionalism_\(art\) "Regionalism (art)").[^324] During the 1960s and 1970s many artists combined traditional Māori and Western techniques, creating unique art forms.[^325] New Zealand art and craft has gradually achieved an international audience, with exhibitions in the [Venice Biennale](https://example.com/wiki/Venice_Biennale "Venice Biennale") in 2001 and the "Paradise Now" exhibition in New York in 2004.[^317][^326] 

[![Refer to caption](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Hinepare.jpg/170px-Hinepare.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Hinepare.jpg)

Portrait of Hinepare of [Ngāti Kahungunu](https://example.com/wiki/Ng%C4%81ti_Kahungunu "Ngāti Kahungunu") by [Gottfried Lindauer](https://example.com/wiki/Gottfried_Lindauer "Gottfried Lindauer"), showing chin [moko](https://example.com/wiki/T%C4%81_moko "Tā moko"), [pounamu](https://example.com/wiki/Pounamu "Pounamu") [hei-tiki](https://example.com/wiki/Hei-tiki "Hei-tiki") and woven cloak 

Māori cloaks are made of fine flax fibre and patterned with black, red and white triangles, diamonds and other geometric shapes.[^327] [Greenstone](https://example.com/wiki/Pounamu "Pounamu") was fashioned into earrings and necklaces, with the most well-known design being the [hei-tiki](https://example.com/wiki/Hei-tiki "Hei-tiki"), a distorted human figure sitting cross-legged with its head tilted to the side.[^328] Europeans brought English fashion etiquette to New Zealand, and until the 1950s most people dressed up for social occasions.[^329] Standards have since relaxed and New Zealand fashion has received a reputation for being casual, practical and lacklustre.[^330][^331] However, the local fashion industry has grown significantly since 2000, doubling exports and increasing from a handful to about 50 established labels, with some labels gaining international recognition.[^331] 

### Literature

Māori quickly adopted writing as a means of sharing ideas, and many of their oral stories and poems were converted to the written form.[^332] Most early English literature was obtained from Britain and it was not until the 1950s when local publishing outlets increased that New Zealand literature started to become widely known.[^333] Although still largely influenced by global trends ([modernism](https://example.com/wiki/Literary_modernism "Literary modernism")) and events (the Great Depression), writers in the 1930s began to develop stories increasingly focused on their experiences in New Zealand. During this period literature changed from a [journalistic](https://example.com/wiki/Journalism "Journalism") activity to a more academic pursuit.[^334] Participation in the world wars gave some New Zealand writers a new perspective on New Zealand culture and with the post-war expansion of universities local literature flourished.[^335] Dunedin is a UNESCO [City of Literature](https://example.com/wiki/City_of_Literature "City of Literature").[^336] 

### Media and entertainment

New Zealand music has been influenced by [blues](https://example.com/wiki/Blues "Blues"), [jazz](https://example.com/wiki/Jazz "Jazz"), [country](https://example.com/wiki/Country_music "Country music"), [rock and roll](https://example.com/wiki/Rock_and_roll "Rock and roll") and [hip hop](https://example.com/wiki/Hip_hop_music "Hip hop music"), with many of these genres given a unique New Zealand interpretation.[^337] Māori developed traditional chants and songs from their ancient Southeast Asian origins, and after centuries of isolation created a unique "monotonous" and "[doleful](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/doleful "wikt:doleful")" sound.[^338] Flutes and trumpets were used as musical instruments[^339] or as signalling devices during war or special occasions.[^340] Early settlers brought over their ethnic music, with [brass bands](https://example.com/wiki/Brass_band "Brass band") and [choral music](https://example.com/wiki/Choir "Choir") being popular, and musicians began touring New Zealand in the 1860s.[^341][^342] [Pipe bands](https://example.com/wiki/Pipe_band "Pipe band") became widespread during the early 20th century.[^343] The New Zealand recording industry began to develop from 1940 onwards and many New Zealand musicians have obtained success in Britain and the United States.[^337] Some artists release Māori language songs and the Māori tradition-based art of *[kapa haka](https://example.com/wiki/Kapa_haka "Kapa haka")* (song and dance) has made a resurgence.[^344] The [New Zealand Music Awards](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Music_Awards "New Zealand Music Awards") are held annually by [Recorded Music NZ](https://example.com/wiki/Recorded_Music_NZ "Recorded Music NZ"); the awards were first held in 1965 by [Reckitt & Colman](https://example.com/wiki/Reckitt_%26_Colman "Reckitt & Colman") as the [Loxene Golden Disc](https://example.com/wiki/Loxene_Golden_Disc "Loxene Golden Disc") awards.[^345] Recorded Music NZ also publishes the country's [official weekly record charts](https://example.com/wiki/Official_New_Zealand_Music_Chart "Official New Zealand Music Chart").[^346] 

[![Hills with inset, round doors. Reflected in water.](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Hobbit_holes_reflected_in_water.jpg/220px-Hobbit_holes_reflected_in_water.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Hobbit_holes_reflected_in_water.jpg)

The [Hobbiton Movie Set](https://example.com/wiki/Hobbiton_Movie_Set "Hobbiton Movie Set"), located near [Matamata](https://example.com/wiki/Matamata "Matamata"), was used for *The Lord of the Rings* film trilogy.[^347] 

Public [radio](https://example.com/wiki/Radio_in_New_Zealand "Radio in New Zealand") was introduced in New Zealand in 1922.[^348] A state-owned [television service](https://example.com/wiki/Television_in_New_Zealand "Television in New Zealand") began in 1960.[^349] Deregulation in the 1980s saw a sudden increase in the numbers of radio and television stations.[^350] New Zealand television primarily broadcasts American and British programming, along with a large number of Australian and local shows.[^351] The number of [New Zealand films](https://example.com/wiki/List_of_New_Zealand_films "List of New Zealand films") significantly increased during the 1970s. In 1978 the [New Zealand Film Commission](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_Film_Commission "New Zealand Film Commission") started assisting local film-makers and many films attained a world audience, some receiving international acknowledgement.[^350] The highest-grossing New Zealand films are *[Hunt for the Wilderpeople](https://example.com/wiki/Hunt_for_the_Wilderpeople "Hunt for the Wilderpeople")*, *[Boy](https://example.com/wiki/Boy_\(2010_film\) "Boy (2010 film)")*, *[The World's Fastest Indian](https://example.com/wiki/The_World%27s_Fastest_Indian "The World's Fastest Indian")*, *[Once Were Warriors](https://example.com/wiki/Once_Were_Warriors_\(film\) "Once Were Warriors (film)")* and *[Whale Rider](https://example.com/wiki/Whale_Rider "Whale Rider")*.[^352] The country's diverse scenery and compact size, plus government incentives,[^353] have encouraged some [producers](https://example.com/wiki/Film_producer "Film producer") to shoot big-budget productions in New Zealand, including *[Avatar](https://example.com/wiki/Avatar_\(2009_film\) "Avatar (2009 film)")*, *[The Lord of the Rings](https://example.com/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_\(film_series\) "The Lord of the Rings (film series)")*, *[The Hobbit](https://example.com/wiki/The_Hobbit_\(film_series\) "The Hobbit (film series)")*, *[The Chronicles of Narnia](https://example.com/wiki/The_Chronicles_of_Narnia_\(film_series\) "The Chronicles of Narnia (film series)")*, *[King Kong](https://example.com/wiki/King_Kong_\(2005_film\) "King Kong (2005 film)")* and *[The Last Samurai](https://example.com/wiki/The_Last_Samurai "The Last Samurai")*.[^354] The New Zealand media industry is dominated by a small number of companies, most of which are foreign-owned, although the [state retains ownership](https://example.com/wiki/Crown_entity "Crown entity") of some television and radio stations.[^355] Since 1994, [Freedom House](https://example.com/wiki/Freedom_House "Freedom House") has consistently ranked New Zealand's press freedom in the top twenty, with the 19th freest media in 2015.[^356] 

### Sports

[![Rugby team wearing all black, facing the camera, knees bent, and facing toward a team wearing white](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Haka_2006.jpg/220px-Haka_2006.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Haka_2006.jpg)

A [haka](https://example.com/wiki/Haka_\(sports\) "Haka (sports)") performed by the [national rugby union team](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_national_rugby_union_team "New Zealand national rugby union team") before a game. The haka is a challenge with vigorous movements and stamping of the feet. 

Most of the major sporting codes played in New Zealand have British origins.[^357] [Rugby union](https://example.com/wiki/Rugby_union "Rugby union") is considered the [national sport](https://example.com/wiki/National_sport "National sport")[^358] and attracts the most spectators.[^359] [Golf](https://example.com/wiki/Golf "Golf"), [netball](https://example.com/wiki/Netball "Netball"), [tennis](https://example.com/wiki/Tennis "Tennis") and [cricket](https://example.com/wiki/Cricket "Cricket") have the highest rates of adult participation, while netball, rugby union and [football (soccer)](https://example.com/wiki/Association_football "Association football") are particularly popular among young people.[^359][^360] Around 54% of New Zealand adolescents participate in sports for their school.[^360] Victorious rugby tours to Australia and the United Kingdom in the [late 1880s](https://example.com/wiki/1888%E2%80%9389_New_Zealand_Native_football_team "1888–89 New Zealand Native football team") and the [early 1900s](https://example.com/wiki/The_Original_All_Blacks "The Original All Blacks") played an early role in instilling a national identity.[^361] [Horseracing](https://example.com/wiki/Horseracing_in_New_Zealand "Horseracing in New Zealand") was also a popular [spectator sport](https://example.com/wiki/Spectator_sport "Spectator sport") and became part of the "Rugby, Racing and Beer" culture during the 1960s.[^362] Māori participation in European sports was particularly evident in rugby and the country's team performs a [haka](https://example.com/wiki/Haka_\(sports\) "Haka (sports)"), a traditional Māori challenge, before international matches.[^363] New Zealand is known for its [extreme sports](https://example.com/wiki/Extreme_sport "Extreme sport"), [adventure tourism](https://example.com/wiki/Adventure_travel "Adventure travel")[^364] and strong [mountaineering](https://example.com/wiki/Mountaineering "Mountaineering") tradition, as seen in the success of notable New Zealander [Sir Edmund Hillary](https://example.com/wiki/Edmund_Hillary "Edmund Hillary").[^365][^366] Other outdoor pursuits such as [cycling](https://example.com/wiki/Cycling_in_New_Zealand "Cycling in New Zealand"), fishing, swimming, running, [tramping](https://example.com/wiki/Tramping_in_New_Zealand "Tramping in New Zealand"), canoeing, hunting, snowsports, surfing and sailing are also popular.[^367] The Polynesian sport of [waka ama](https://example.com/wiki/Waka_ama "Waka ama") racing has experienced a resurgence of interest in New Zealand since the 1980s.[^368] 

New Zealand has competitive international teams in [rugby union](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_national_rugby_union_team "New Zealand national rugby union team"), [rugby league](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_national_rugby_league_team "New Zealand national rugby league team"), [netball](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_national_netball_team "New Zealand national netball team"), [cricket](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_national_cricket_team "New Zealand national cricket team"), [softball](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_men%27s_national_softball_team "New Zealand men's national softball team"), and [sailing](https://example.com/wiki/Team_New_Zealand "Team New Zealand"). New Zealand participated at the [Summer Olympics](https://example.com/wiki/Summer_Olympics "Summer Olympics") in 1908 and 1912 as [a joint team with Australia](https://example.com/wiki/Australasia_at_the_Olympics "Australasia at the Olympics"), before first participating [on its own](https://example.com/wiki/New_Zealand_at_the_Olympics "New Zealand at the Olympics") in 1920.[^369] The country has ranked highly on a medals-to-population ratio at recent Games.[^370][^371] The "All Blacks", the national rugby union team, are the most successful in the history of international rugby[^372] and the reigning [World Cup](https://example.com/wiki/Rugby_World_Cup "Rugby World Cup") champions.[^373] 

### Cuisine

[![Raw meat and vegetables](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Hangi_ingredients.jpg/220px-Hangi_ingredients.jpg)](https://example.com/wiki/File:Hangi_ingredients.jpg)

Ingredients to be prepared for a [hāngi](https://example.com/wiki/H%C4%81ngi "Hāngi") 

The national cuisine has been described as [Pacific Rim](https://example.com/wiki/Pacific_Rim "Pacific Rim"), incorporating the native [Māori cuisine](https://example.com/wiki/M%C4%81ori_cuisine "Māori cuisine") and diverse culinary traditions introduced by settlers and immigrants from Europe, Polynesia and Asia.[^374] New Zealand yields produce from land and sea—most crops and livestock, such as maize, potatoes and pigs, were gradually introduced by the early European settlers.[^375] Distinctive ingredients or dishes include [lamb](https://example.com/wiki/Lamb_and_mutton "Lamb and mutton"), salmon, [kōura](https://example.com/wiki/K%C5%8Dura "Kōura") (crayfish),[^376] [dredge oysters](https://example.com/wiki/Dredge_oyster "Dredge oyster"), [whitebait](https://example.com/wiki/Whitebait "Whitebait"), [pāua](https://example.com/wiki/P%C4%81ua "Pāua") (abalone), mussels, scallops, [pipis](https://example.com/wiki/Paphies_australis "Paphies australis") and [tuatua](https://example.com/wiki/Tuatua "Tuatua") (both are types of New Zealand shellfish),[^377] [kūmara](https://example.com/wiki/K%C5%ABmara "Kūmara") (sweet potato), [kiwifruit](https://example.com/wiki/Kiwifruit "Kiwifruit"), [tamarillo](https://example.com/wiki/Tamarillo "Tamarillo") and [pavlova](https://example.com/wiki/Pavlova_\(food\) "Pavlova (food)") (considered a national dish).[^378][^374] A [hāngi](https://example.com/wiki/H%C4%81ngi "Hāngi") is a traditional Māori method of cooking food using heated rocks buried in a pit oven. After European colonisation, Māori began cooking with pots and ovens and the hāngi was used less frequently, although it is still used for formal occasions such as [tangihanga](https://example.com/wiki/Tangihanga "Tangihanga").[^379]
